This is a guide for someone planning a first trip to Bali. Most of the friction is in two things: figuring out which area of the island to base out of, and managing the gap between what Bali looks like on a map (small and navigable) and what it actually feels like to move around (slow and traffic-heavy). The rest sorts itself out.

## Key facts

- **Currency:** Indonesian Rupiah (IDR)
- **Language:** Bahasa Indonesia
- **Population:** roughly 4.3 million on the island
- **Climate:** tropical, with a wet season November through March and a dry season April through October
- **Time zone:** Central Indonesia Time (WITA), UTC+8
- **Visa:** many nationalities can get a 30-day visa on arrival, with extensions available; check the specific rule for your passport before you fly

## Stuff you should know before you land

Do not overpack. Affordable laundry is everywhere on the island, often with same-day or next-day pickup for a few euros per kilogram.

Drug laws are severe. Possession can carry the death penalty. This is not the country to bring anything in or take anything you cannot identify.

Bali looks small from a European or American perspective, but the roads are congested and an hour from the airport to your hotel is normal.

The sun sets in the west. If a sunset view from your room matters to you, pick the area accordingly.

Greet people with a slight bow and a smile, especially when entering a home or a small shop. Take your shoes off when entering a home or a temple.

After the rice harvest you will see flocks of ducks moving through the paddies. Farmers use them to plow the soil, eat the pests, and fertilize the field.

Watch where you walk. Shopkeepers leave small offerings of flowers and incense on the sidewalk in front of their stores. They are easy to step on or trip over.

## Planning the trip

### When to go

The dry season runs April through October and offers the most reliable beach and outdoor weather. The wet season (November through March) is greener and quieter, with lower prices and more rain. Either window can work depending on what you want.

### How to get there

The main entry point is Ngurah Rai International Airport (DPS) in Denpasar. There are direct flights from many Asian cities, Australia, and increasingly from Europe.

From the airport: a metered taxi to Kuta or Seminyak runs roughly IDR 150,000 to 300,000. Pre-booked airport transfers are usually more reliable than hailing on arrival, especially with a group; this is what I do most often. Grab and Gojek are the rideshare apps on Bali. Uber does not operate here.

You can also reach Bali by ferry from Lombok, Nusa Penida, or Java.

## Where to stay

Bali looks like a grid on a map but does not actually function as one. The main roads are long and do not always connect; if you book separate accommodations across the island, check the actual driving and walking times between them before assuming you can move easily.

| Area | Best for | Caveats |
|---|---|---|
| Ubud | Culture, wellness, rice terraces | Inland; not on the coast |
| Seminyak | Restaurants, shopping, walkable | Touristy; gets pricey |
| Canggu | Surf, cafes, slower pace, remote work | Traffic on the main road has gotten worse |
| Kuta | Nightlife, budget travel | Not where to base for rest |
| Nusa Dua | Calm beaches, resort comfort | Gated atmosphere; crowd skews older |
| Sanur | Family-friendly, quiet | Less to do at night |
| Uluwatu | Cliff coast, surf, sea temple | Spread out; need transport |

A few notes on these in my own ranking. Canggu is where I usually settle for a few weeks when I am on the island; the surf and cafe density work for the way I like to travel. Ubud is the right base for a calmer, less coastal trip. Kuta is fun for a night out and not where I would actually stay.

## Towns and regions

### Denpasar

The administrative capital, often skipped by visitors. Worth a half-day if you want a closer look at how Balinese life runs day to day. Badung Market is the busy traditional market. The Bali Museum gives a quick run through the island's history. Puputan Square commemorates the resistance to colonial occupation.

### Ubud

The cultural center. The Sacred Monkey Forest is a sanctuary and temple complex with hundreds of macaques living among the structures. The Tegallalang rice terraces north of town are the most-photographed terraces on the island. The Ubud Palace and the adjacent art market sit in the center, and a wide range of yoga and wellness studios run classes and retreats.

### Seminyak

The trendier beach base. Seminyak Beach itself is good for an afternoon and decent for surfing or paddleboarding. Eat Street (Jalan Kayu Aya) is the strip with the densest concentration of restaurants and boutiques. The beach clubs (Ku De Ta and Potato Head are the well-known ones) run from afternoon through sunset.

### Canggu

The more laid-back beach scene. Batu Bolong Beach is the standard surf spot with bars in the sand. Tanah Lot, the sea temple at sunset, is a short drive north. The cafe and coworking density is what makes Canggu the stop for remote workers.

### Kuta

The party stretch. Kuta Beach has gentle waves for beginning surfers. Waterbom Bali is a large water park if you have kids. The nightlife is the main draw; the area is tourist-saturated otherwise.

### Nusa Dua

The high-end resort enclave. Geger Beach is calm and well-maintained. The Bali National Golf Club and the Devdan Show (a cultural performance at the Bali Nusa Dua Theatre) are the two named attractions. The atmosphere is gated, which has its own appeal if that is what you are after.

### Sanur

The family-friendly base. Sanur Beach is long, flat, and calm. The Le Mayeur Museum is a small museum dedicated to the Belgian painter who lived here. Several night markets run after sunset.

### Uluwatu

The cliff coast. Uluwatu Temple sits on a promontory above the Indian Ocean and is one of the most important religious sites on the island. Padang Padang Beach below is popular with surfers. Single Fin Bar is the cliffside spot for sunset.

### Jimbaran

Known for seafood and sunsets. Jimbaran Beach is calm and the sand is golden. The Jimbaran Fish Market is busy in the early morning if you want to see the fresh catch. Several beachside restaurants serve grilled seafood directly on the sand. Rock Bar at the AYANA resort is the well-known sunset spot.

### Amed

A quiet stretch on the east coast with black sand beaches, coral reefs, and a clear view of Mount Agung. The diving and snorkeling here are among the better on the island, including a Japanese shipwreck near Tulamben. Amed is also a base for trekking Mount Agung.

### Sidemen

A countryside escape in East Bali, set among rice terraces and mountains. Walks through the terraces, weaving workshops, and uninterrupted views of Mount Agung at sunrise.

### Lovina

On the north coast, with black sand beaches and dolphin-watching boat trips. Banjar Hot Springs is a short drive away. Gitgit Waterfall is in the surrounding jungle.

## What I would not skip

**Tanah Lot Temple.** Show up 60 to 90 minutes before sunset, browse the stalls (the Bintang tank tops are a bit cliché but cheap), and find a table at one of the restaurants with a view of the temple. Do not wait until the sun is dropping to look for seating.

**The Tegallalang rice terraces.** A UNESCO World Heritage site north of Ubud. I usually do not recommend paying for tours, but this is a case where hiring a driver is worth it.

## Other things worth doing

Surf at Uluwatu, Padang Padang, or Kuta Beach. Book a Balinese spa treatment at any reputable place. Hike Mount Batur for sunrise. Visit an ethical coffee plantation; Ecobike Cafe runs a good tour. Avoid Luwak coffee on principle (the civets that produce it are caged and force-fed). See a traditional dance performance, either the Kecak at Uluwatu Temple or the Legong in Ubud. Visit one of the named waterfalls (Sekumpul, Tegenungan, or Aling-Aling). Take a yoga class in Ubud. Day-trip or stay overnight on Nusa Penida for Kelingking Beach and Angel's Billabong.
