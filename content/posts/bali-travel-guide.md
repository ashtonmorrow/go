Bali is not hard to enjoy, but it is easy to plan badly. The usual mistake is treating it like a small island where you can sleep anywhere and casually cross the map.

A beach, a rice terrace, a temple, and dinner somewhere else can look like a sensible day until the road turns it into hours in traffic. Bali works better when you choose the kind of day you want to repeat, stay near it, and resist the urge to stitch the island together one photo stop at a time.

## Choose the Kind of Day You Want to Repeat

I would not pick a Bali base by asking which town is "best." I would ask what kind of day you want to repeat.

| Base | What it is good for | The trade-off |
|---|---|---|
| Ubud | Rice fields, temples, art markets, dance, yoga, slower mornings | Inland, so beach days require a drive |
| Canggu | Surf, cafes, longer stays, remote work | Traffic has become part of the experience |
| Seminyak | Restaurants, boutiques, beach access | Polished, pricey, and heavily touristed |
| Sanur | Families, calm water, a walkable beach path | Quieter at night |
| Nusa Dua | Resort comfort and low-friction beach days | Gated and detached from daily Balinese life |
| Uluwatu | Cliffs, surf, temple sunsets | Spread out, so you need transport |
| Jimbaran | Seafood dinners, calmer sand, airport convenience | Better for a night or two than a whole trip |
| Amed, Sidemen, Lovina | Slower east and north Bali | Too far for a rushed first visit |

For a first trip, I would usually split time between Ubud and one coastal base. Ubud gives you rice fields, temple courtyards, gamelan and dance performances, small museums, and day trips into the hills. Then choose the coast by temperament. Sanur is the gentle option. Canggu is better for surf and cafes if you can tolerate traffic. Uluwatu is for cliffs and surf breaks. Nusa Dua is the resort answer, which is sometimes exactly what people want and sometimes the wrong version of Bali entirely.

## Sort Out the Arrival

Most visitors arrive at Ngurah Rai International Airport (DPS), just south of Denpasar. Many nationalities can use Indonesia's B1 visa on arrival or e-VOA for 30 days, with one possible 30-day extension. Check the official immigration site before relying on that, because visa rules are the kind of thing that can quietly ruin a good plan.

Pre-book the first airport transfer if you are arriving tired or with luggage. Grab and Gojek are the main ride-hailing apps on the island; Uber does not operate here. A ride from the airport to Kuta or Seminyak can run roughly IDR 150,000 to 300,000, about $9 to $18, but pickup rules and traffic can make arrival messier than the price suggests.

Do not overpack. Laundry is cheap and easy to arrange through hotels, guesthouses, and street-side laundries, often with same-day or next-day return. Do not bring anything ambiguous in your luggage. Indonesian drug laws are severe, and this is not a place to be casual about what is in a bag.

The small cultural details matter. Offerings of flowers, leaves, and incense sit on sidewalks and thresholds. Step around them. Take your shoes off when entering homes and temples. Carry a sarong or light layer for sacred sites. The point is not to perform reverence perfectly. It is to avoid behaving as if the island is only a backdrop.

## Use Ubud for the Inland Trip

Ubud is busy now, and some of the center feels more arranged for visitors than I would like. I would still use it as a first inland base because it puts several kinds of Bali close together. You can walk rice-field paths in the morning, visit a small museum or market in the afternoon, and see a dance or gamelan performance in the evening without needing to cross the whole island.

The [Sacred Monkey Forest](/pins/sacred-monkey-forest-sanctuary) is both a temple complex and an animal habitat, not just a place to photograph macaques. Keep food out of sight, give the animals space, and watch what other tourists are doing mostly as a lesson in what not to copy.

The Tegallalang rice terraces north of town are heavily photographed, but they make more sense if you think of them as working irrigation landscape. Bali's subak system links water, fields, temples, and farmer obligations into a cooperative structure. You do not need a technical lecture to see the difference. Once you notice the channels and field edges, the terraces stop being only a view.

After harvest, you may see ducks moving through the paddies. Farmers use them to eat pests, disturb the soil, and fertilize the fields. That kind of detail is why I would rather spend a slow morning in the fields than chase three more famous stops.

## Choose the Coast by Temperament

The south coast is where many Bali trips become either easy or irritating. Seminyak has restaurants, shopping, and a more polished beach scene. Canggu is where I would settle for a longer working stay because the cafe density and surf rhythm suit that kind of trip, but the traffic has become hard to ignore. Kuta has budget nightlife and beginner waves, and I would not base there for rest.

Sanur is underrated for a first visit if you want calm water, a flat beach path, and less drama. Nusa Dua is comfortable and controlled. I would not stay there to understand the island, but I would stay there if the assignment was a low-friction beach reset. Uluwatu is more dramatic: cliffs, surf, Uluwatu Temple, and sunset. The cost is that everything is spread out.

Jimbaran is useful near the end of a trip. The beach is calmer, the airport is close, and grilled seafood on the sand is exactly as straightforward as it sounds. If your flight leaves the next day, that is not a bad final night.

## Save the East and North for More Time

Amed, Sidemen, and Lovina are the places I would add when the trip is long enough to stop rushing. Amed sits on the east coast with black sand, coral, diving, snorkeling, and views toward Mount Agung when the weather cooperates. Sidemen is inland and quieter, with rice terrace walks and weaving workshops. Lovina, on the north coast, moves at a different pace from the south but takes long enough to reach that I would not squeeze it into a short first trip.

These places are not more convenient. They are simply less consumed by the main tourism loop.

## What I Would Actually Prioritize

I would hire a driver for one temple and rice-field day rather than pretend the island is easy to stitch together on my own. [Tanah Lot](/pins/tanah-lot) is the obvious sunset temple. Uluwatu Temple has the cliff setting and the evening Kecak performance, a dance-drama built around a large seated chorus whose repeated vocal patterns replace instrumental accompaniment.

I would see one performance in Ubud. Gamelan is the Balinese percussion ensemble tradition built around metallophones, gongs, drums, and interlocking rhythmic figures. You can listen without knowing the theory. The simplest thing to notice is how fast repeating patterns sit inside slower gong cycles.

I would choose beaches by use, not fame. Sanur for calm water. Uluwatu or Padang Padang for surf and cliffs. Jimbaran for seafood at sunset. Amed for snorkeling and diving. The famous beach is not always the right beach.

## What I Would Handle Carefully

Luwak coffee is an easy skip. The civets used to produce it are often caged and force-fed, and the ethical versions are difficult to verify casually.

Mount Batur sunrise hikes can be good in clear weather, but they require a very early start and they are crowded. They are not the quiet nature experience some tours imply.

Nusa Penida is visually dramatic and physically annoying. Roads are rough, day trips are rushed, and the photo stops can feel like lines arranged around cliffs. Stay overnight if you really want to go, or save it for a second trip.

## When to Go

The dry season, roughly April through October, is the safer window for beaches, scooters, rice terrace walks, and outdoor day trips. The wet season, roughly November through March, is greener and often cheaper, but rain can turn a transfer into the main event of the afternoon.

The island is hot year-round. The practical choice is not summer versus winter. It is whether you want more reliable beach weather or a quieter, wetter version of the same place.
