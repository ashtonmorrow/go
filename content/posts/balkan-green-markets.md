I have watched a lot of American travelers wander into a green market in Croatia or Bosnia and walk back out within ten minutes. The produce is wonky and dusty, prices are not posted, and the older sellers behind the tables are not interested in performing for tourists. The piles of perfect-looking tomatoes that did meet expectations turn out to be from resellers, not farmers.

I learned how to actually use one of these markets in Split, when my host walked me through Pazar, the main market on the edge of Diocletian's Palace. What follows is what she showed me, plus what I have watched work since.

## How to read a stall

The piles matter. Skip the tables stacked high with uniform produce. Those are resellers buying wholesale and remarketing as local. The sellers worth your money have a small, mixed table: six cucumbers, ten tomatoes, a bunch of swiss chard, two aubergines, a handful of potatoes. The mix is whatever was ready to pick that morning on a small plot, and the quantity ends when the table empties. If the table looks like more than one family could grow, it probably is.

## Pick your own produce

If you let the seller fill the bag, expect a few bruised pieces they were trying to move. Ask for a bag (or hand over your own), then pick the items yourself before handing the bag back to be weighed. Tell them what you want first. Approaching the table and starting to handle produce without a word reads as rude.

## Time of day matters

The good stalls are picked over by mid-morning. Most markets open around 6am, and by noon what is left is the picked-over remainder. If you are serious about buying, go before nine.

## A few rules I broke at first

Do not haggle. The Balkans are not a haggling culture for produce, prices are already low, and this is the seller's livelihood. Do not pose with the produce for a photo and then put it back without buying. The sellers see this constantly and dislike it. Pay in cash, in local currency, in small denominations. The old-school spring scales most stalls use are not precise enough to make change easy.

If your purchase comes out to an awkward total, the seller will often try to sell you whatever is left on the table to round up to a clean note. Sometimes this is a real bargain. Sometimes it is the produce that did not move all morning. Decide based on the produce, not the convenience.

## Look at the ground

Outside the formal market you will see older people selling small piles of produce off a blanket or tarp on the sidewalk. These are usually retired farmers or villagers with a small surplus, not full-time vendors. The produce is almost certainly pesticide-free because they are not commercial growers, and pensions for retired farmers in this part of the world are thin. Buy from them. The cherries I have eaten from these sellers have been better than anything I bought inside the market itself.

## What else is worth buying

Most green markets carry homemade goods that are technically gray-market: pickled cabbage scooped out of an open plastic barrel, fresh honey in unmarked jars, soft cheeses, prosciutto, and rakija. Hygiene standards are looser than what you may be used to. The standard local view is that homemade is better than industrial, and the people selling these things eat them themselves. Use your own judgment.

If you are renting an Airbnb and your host offers to introduce you to someone who makes their own olive oil or liqueur, take the introduction. The pricing is usually informal, the quality is higher than what you find at a supermarket, and you will leave with stories you would not have had otherwise.
