The green markets across Croatia, Slovenia, Bosnia and Herzegovina, Serbia, and Montenegro all run on the same logic. Read one and you can walk into any of the others and orient yourself in thirty seconds. What changes between them is how touristed each one is, and how much that changes what's on the tables.

I rotate through several markets on a typical multi-week trip. The first one I learned was Pazar in [Split](/cities/split), on the edge of Diocletian's Palace. It now sits closer to half tourist market than full local one. Sellers perform for cameras, more of the produce comes from resellers, and a fair number of stalls price for visitors. The advice below applies less cleanly there than it used to.

For the cleaner version, the market in [Bar, Montenegro](/cities/bar-mne) is a better example: almost no English spoken, no posted prices, and what's on the table came from someone's plot that morning.

What follows applies cleanly at Bar-style markets and partially at Pazar.

## Spot the real growers

Skip the tables stacked high with uniform produce. Those are resellers buying wholesale and re-presenting as local. The sellers worth your money have a small, mixed table:

- Six cucumbers
- Ten tomatoes
- A bunch of swiss chard
- Two aubergines
- A handful of potatoes

The mix is whatever was ready to pick that morning on a small plot. Quantity ends when the table is empty. If the table looks like more than one family could grow, it probably is.

## Pick your own produce

If you let the seller fill the bag, expect a few bruised pieces they were trying to move. Better:

1. Tell them what you want.
2. Ask for a bag, or hand over your own.
3. Pick the items yourself.
4. Hand the bag back to be weighed.

Approaching the table and starting to handle produce without a word reads as rude.

## Go before nine

Most markets open around 6am. The good stalls are picked over by mid-morning, and by noon what's left is the picked-over remainder. Show up before nine if you are serious about buying.

If you stay in one place for several weeks, this gets easier. Hit the same stall enough mornings in a row and the seller starts setting things aside for you.

## Rules I broke at first

A short list of things I figured out the hard way:

- **Don't haggle.** The Balkans are not a haggling culture for produce. Prices are already low and this is the seller's livelihood.
- **Don't pose with the produce for a photo and then put it back.** Sellers see it constantly and it lands badly.
- **Pay in cash, in local currency, in small denominations.** The old-school spring scales most stalls use are not precise enough to make change easy.

If your purchase comes out to an awkward total, the seller will often try to round up to a clean note by tossing in whatever is left on the table. Sometimes that's a real bargain. Sometimes it's the produce that did not move all morning. Decide based on the produce, not the convenience.

## Look at the ground

Outside the formal market you will see older people selling small piles of produce off a blanket or tarp on the sidewalk. These are usually retired farmers or villagers with a small surplus, not full-time vendors. Two reasons to buy from them:

- The produce is almost certainly pesticide-free. They are not commercial growers.
- Pensions for retired farmers in this part of the world are thin.

The pensioners tend to have whatever is in season that week. They are selling out of their own garden, so what's on the blanket is whatever ripened. That makes it the freshest thing on offer, and often the best single buy of the morning.

## What else is worth buying

Most green markets carry homemade goods that are technically gray-market:

- Pickled cabbage scooped from an open plastic barrel
- Fresh honey in unmarked jars
- Soft cheeses
- Prosciutto
- Rakija

Hygiene standards are looser than what you may be used to. The local view is that homemade is better than industrial, and the people selling these things eat them themselves. Use your own judgment.

If you are renting an apartment for more than a few days and your host offers to introduce you to someone who makes their own olive oil or liqueur, take the introduction. Pricing is informal, the quality is higher than what a supermarket sells, and you will leave with stories.

## A note for cat people

The markets are reliable places to pet cats. The same animals show up most mornings, the fish stalls draw a crowd of them, and the older sellers usually keep a handful around the edges of the produce tables. If you, like me, route your morning around chances to scratch a cat behind the ears, both Bar and Split deliver.
