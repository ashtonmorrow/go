I lived and worked out of Alicante for six or seven weeks, and several friends came through while I was there. The pattern surprised me: travelers who know Spain well enough to plan Madrid, Barcelona, Valencia, Seville, or Granada often skip Alicante entirely.

I think that is usually a mistake. Alicante is not the city I would build a whole first Spain trip around, but it is one of the easiest coastal stops to add. It has a real old town, a castle above the center, practical rail links, good food, and beaches that do not require a complicated resort plan.

## Why It Works as a Stop

Alicante is useful because it does not ask for much. Two or three days is enough for the city, the castle, a beach afternoon, and a short side trip. It also fits neatly between larger places: Madrid by high-speed rail, Valencia up the coast, or a low-cost flight from much of Europe.

That makes it different from some Spanish beach destinations. It is not only a resort base, and it is not only a day trip. You can arrive by train, walk into the old town, swim near the center, eat well, and leave without renting a car.

## Getting There From Madrid, Valencia, or Barcelona

Most travelers from the Americas or Asia land at Madrid (MAD) or Barcelona (BCN). On a map both look about equidistant from Alicante. In practice Madrid is the easier connection by rail.

From Madrid, the Renfe AVE high-speed train runs Madrid Atocha to Alicante in roughly 2 hours 20 minutes. IRYO, the private operator that began service in late 2022, also runs the same route now; older travel writeups still describe IRYO as forthcoming, but it has been operational for a couple of years. Comparing both for a specific date usually surfaces the better fare.

From Valencia, the AVE is around 1 hour 35 minutes. Worth knowing if you are already on the eastern coast and considering whether to add Alicante to a trip already planned around Valencia.

From Barcelona by rail, the journey is longer (around 5 hours), so most visitors fly that segment.

You can also fly direct. Alicante-Elche Airport (ALC) is well-served by European low-cost carriers (Ryanair, Vueling, easyJet) and is often the simplest option from London, Paris, Amsterdam, or any of the German hubs. I do not know of nonstop transatlantic service to ALC, so for travelers from the Americas the rail-from-Madrid path remains the standard.

## Choosing the Right Beach

Alicante's beaches break into a few categories, and the best choice depends on the day you want.

**For a beach inside the city.** [Postiguet Beach](/pins/platja-del-postiguet) is the urban beach, right at the foot of the old town and the [Castle of Santa Bárbara](/pins/santa-barbara-castle), with showers, kiosks, and lifeguards. It is convenient and decent for a short visit, but it gets crowded.

**For a longer sand beach.** San Juan Beach, north of the center, is the destination beach if you have a half-day to swim or want the more resort-feeling stretch.

**For clearer water and fewer people.** [Cala Cantalar](/pins/cala-cantalar) and Cala Palmera, north along the coast, are smaller and quieter. The water is clearer than at the urban beaches; the trade-off is that you walk in over rocks rather than sand.

**For windsurfing.** [El Campello](/pins/el-campello), a short tram ride north of Alicante, has the conditions and the schools.

## What to Eat

The bar and restaurant scene is one of the reasons Alicante works better than its reputation. Local specialties to seek out: arroz a banda, a rice cooked in fish broth; gazpacho alicantino, the meat-and-flatbread version rather than the cold tomato soup of Andalucía; and turrón, the almond-honey nougat with the protected designation centered nearby in Jijona.

The general advice that applies in any Spanish city applies here. Order tapas at the bar instead of sit-down menus. Eat dinner late; most kitchens do not get going before 9pm. Avoid the restaurants directly on the Explanada de España with menus translated into seven languages.

## The Old Town, the Castle, and the Museums

Alicante's old town (the Casco Antiguo, also called Santa Cruz) sits between the harbor and the hill that holds the castle. The streets are narrow, the houses are painted, and the bars are dense. Walk it in the early evening before dinner.

The Castle of Santa Bárbara sits on Mount Benacantil, 166 meters above the city. The walk up is doable but warm in summer; an elevator from the seafront is also accessible through a tunnel. The castle itself is medieval, Moorish in origin and expanded later. The main draw is the panoramic view from the upper levels rather than the interior.

Two museums are worth a stop if you want a break from the beach circuit: MARQ, the provincial archaeology museum, and MACA, a smaller contemporary art museum.

## How Long to Stay

Two to three full days is usually right. One day for the city, one day for the beaches, and an optional day for Elche, the [UNESCO-listed palm grove](/pins/palmeral-of-elche), or another stop down the coast.

If the trip is longer, I would slot Alicante between Madrid and Valencia, or use it as a coastal reset before continuing south. It is not the most dramatic city in Spain. It is simply easier, warmer, and more rewarding than many travelers expect.
