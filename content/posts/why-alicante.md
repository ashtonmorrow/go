I lived and worked out of Alicante for six or seven weeks recently, and several friends came to visit on my recommendation. The pattern surprised me: most of the American and northern European travelers I know plan trips around Madrid, Barcelona, and the Andalusian cities, and almost none of them have Alicante on the list. After a month and a half of being there, I think that is a mistake. It does not demand a week, but it is genuinely good for two or three days, and it slots cleanly into a longer Spain itinerary.

## Connecting from a transcontinental flight

Most travelers from the Americas or Asia land at Madrid (MAD) or Barcelona (BCN). On a map both look about equidistant from Alicante. In practice Madrid is the easier connection by rail.

From Madrid, the Renfe AVE high-speed train runs Madrid Atocha to Alicante in roughly 2 hours 20 minutes. IRYO, the private operator that began service in late 2022, also runs the same route now; older travel writeups still describe IRYO as forthcoming, but it has been operational for a couple of years. Comparing both for a specific date usually surfaces the better fare.

From Valencia, the AVE is around 1 hour 35 minutes. Worth knowing if you are already on the eastern coast and considering whether to add Alicante to a trip already planned around Valencia.

From Barcelona by rail, the journey is longer (around 5 hours), so most visitors fly that segment.

You can also fly direct. Alicante-Elche Airport (ALC) is well-served by European low-cost carriers (Ryanair, Vueling, easyJet) and is often the simplest option from London, Paris, Amsterdam, or any of the German hubs. I do not know of nonstop transatlantic service to ALC, so for travelers from the Americas the rail-from-Madrid path remains the standard.

## The beaches

The reason most visitors come is the coast. Alicante's beaches break into a few categories.

**Sandy beaches inside the city.** Postiguet Beach is the urban beach, right at the foot of the old town and the Castle of Santa Bárbara, with showers, kiosks, and lifeguards. Convenient and decent for a short visit; gets crowded.

**Sandy beaches outside the city.** San Juan Beach, north of the center, is a long wide beach with golden sand. The destination beach if you have a half-day to spend swimming or want the more resort-feeling stretch.

**Coves and rocky beaches.** Cala Cantalar and Cala Palmera, north along the coast, are smaller and quieter. The water is clearer than at the urban beaches; the trade-off is that you walk in over rocks rather than sand.

**Windsurfing.** El Campello, a short tram ride north of Alicante, has the conditions and the schools.

## Food

The bar and restaurant scene is one of the real reasons to be here. Local specialties to seek out: arroz a banda (a rice cooked in fish broth, simpler and more refined than paella), gazpacho alicantino (the meat-and-flatbread version, not the cold tomato soup of Andalucía), and turrón (the almond-honey nougat, with the protected designation centered nearby in Jijona).

The general advice that applies in any Spanish city applies here. Order tapas at the bar instead of sit-down menus. Eat dinner late; most kitchens do not get going before 9pm. Avoid the restaurants directly on the Explanada de España with menus translated into seven languages.

## The old town and the castle

Alicante's old town (the Casco Antiguo, also called Santa Cruz) sits between the harbor and the hill that holds the castle. The streets are narrow, the houses are painted, and the bars are dense. Walk it in the early evening before dinner.

The Castle of Santa Bárbara sits on Mount Benacantil, 166 meters above the city. The walk up is doable but warm in summer; an elevator from the seafront is also accessible through a tunnel. The castle itself is medieval, Moorish in origin and expanded later. The main draw is the panoramic view from the upper levels rather than the interior.

Two museums worth a stop: MARQ (the provincial archaeology museum, well-organized, an hour or so) and MACA (contemporary art, smaller and free).

## How long to stay

Two to three full days is usually right. One full day for the city, one day for the beaches and a short day trip out, and an optional day for Elche (the UNESCO palm grove) or further down the coast. If you are planning a longer Spain trip, slotting Alicante between Madrid and Valencia, or as a stop on the way south to Andalucía, is the cleanest fit.
