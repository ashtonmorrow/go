The Bernina Express had been on my list for years. I was in Milan after a few days in Rome and realized I was a regional train ride away from Tirano, the southern endpoint of the line, so I went. What follows is the practical write-up I wish I had had before I bought my ticket.

## What it actually is

The Bernina Express is the named scenic service operated by Rhaetian Railways (RhB) between Tirano in Italian Lombardy and Chur in the Swiss canton of Graubünden. The route is not high-speed: the train runs slowly through the Alps in panoramic-windowed cars built for sightseeing rather than transit. The full journey takes about four hours.

The route crosses the Bernina Pass at Ospizio Bernina, which sits at 2,253 meters. That makes the Bernina Express the highest adhesion (no rack-and-pinion) railway crossing in the Alps. Higher rack railways exist in Switzerland (the Jungfrau Railway reaches 3,454 meters at the Jungfraujoch, for example), but those are a different mechanical category and most travel writeups conflate the two.

The line was inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage list in 2008 as the Rhaetian Railway in the Albula and Bernina Cultural Landscape. The engineering, including 55 tunnels and 196 bridges across the Albula and Bernina sections, is the reason for the inscription.

The trains themselves are panoramic-window railcars in two classes. First class has a 1+2 seating layout, second class is 2+2. Reclining seats and air conditioning in both. Wifi has been added on some services but is not consistent, so do not plan to work the whole way.

## Booking

Tickets sell out, especially in summer. Book directly from Rhaetian Railways at rhb.ch. Mark Smith's *Man in Seat 61* site has the canonical guide to the cheaper booking paths; if you have lead time, his method through Deutsche Bahn can save real money. I booked less than a week out and bought directly from RhB at full price.

A seat reservation is mandatory on the Bernina Express, so booking ahead is not optional. If you have a Swiss Travel Pass or a Eurail/Interrail pass, the underlying transport is included but you still pay the seat reservation supplement.

## Getting to Tirano from Milan

The Bernina Express does not start in a major city. Most travelers arriving from Italy reach the southern endpoint at Tirano on the regional train from Milano Centrale. The journey is about two and a half hours, dynamic pricing does not apply, and you can buy the ticket from a kiosk at the station the day of.

Validate your ticket before boarding. Italian regional tickets are open-dated until you stamp them, and you stamp them at the small green machines on the platform. I forgot to do this. The conductor was generous and only made me buy a second ticket on the spot rather than levying a fine; he also pointed out that the original ticket I had bought is still good for any future trip on the same route. Useful that one time. Do not count on that grace.

## The route, southbound to northbound

The standard Bernina Express stops, in order from Tirano to Chur:

- Tirano (Italy)
- Poschiavo
- Alp Grüm
- Ospizio Bernina (the high point, 2,253 m)
- Pontresina
- Samedan
- St. Moritz
- Filisur
- Thusis
- Chur

Two stretches in particular are the photographs everyone takes. Between Brusio and Poschiavo the line spirals through the Brusio circular viaduct (a single-track 360-degree loop on a stone bridge). Between Filisur and Thusis the train crosses the Landwasser Viaduct, where the line emerges from a tunnel directly onto a curving stone arch over the river.

If you only have one direction in your itinerary, I would recommend southbound (Chur to Tirano). I rode it northbound and the views were good in either direction, but the Pass is more dramatic when you are climbing toward it than descending from it.

## On Chur

Chur is often described as Switzerland's oldest city. The claim is based on archaeological dating that puts settlement on the site at around 5,000 to 11,000 years (depending on which dig you cite); the precise "oldest continuously inhabited" framing is contested by other Swiss towns. Either way, it is a small, walkable Alpine city worth a half-day if you are ending the journey there. The old town is compact and the train station sits at the edge of it.

## Worth knowing

The Bernina Express run is not the only way to ride this line. The same tracks are used by RhB regional trains between Tirano and St. Moritz, which run more frequently and cost less. They do not have the panoramic cars, but the windows in the regional carriages are large enough that you still see most of what makes the route worth riding. If the Bernina Express is sold out for your dates, the regional service is a reasonable backup.
