If you are planning a first trip to Thailand and do not know where to start, this is the orientation. The country runs more efficiently than most first-time visitors expect, and the friction is concentrated in a few specific areas: getting cash without paying spreads to a money changer, navigating Bangkok without a car, and dressing appropriately for temple visits. The rest is figuring out which cities you want to spend time in.

## Cities at a glance

| City | What it is | Recommended length |
|---|---|---|
| Bangkok | Dense, hot capital. Temples, food, markets, transit | 2 to 4 days |
| Khao Yai | National park, vineyards, mountains. The nature break from Bangkok | 2 to 3 days |
| Chiang Mai | Quieter northern city. Temples, food, slower pace | 4 to 7 days |
| Phuket | Largest island. Beaches from luxury resorts to backpacker bars | 3 to 5 days |
| Koh Samui | Quieter island alternative. Beaches and resorts | 3 to 5 days |

## Tips that apply across Thailand

**Use ATMs for cash.** Money changers usually charge a worse rate than your home bank's ATM withdrawal, even with the standard 3% foreign transaction fee. Check your specific card's policy before you go.

**Do not rent a car to drive in Bangkok.** Download Grab, the local rideshare app (Uber does not operate in Thailand). It lets you pay by credit card, which spares you fumbling for cash in the back of a taxi at night. Grab also delivers food, including pad thai for around three dollars at 2am, and you can hire a scooter through the same app to cross the city. Many Grab drivers also offer private day rates or out-of-city transfers; if you liked your driver, ask for a card.

**Pack layers and modest clothing for temples.** Temples and cultural sites require knees and shoulders covered. The heat can also be punishing, so light layers work better than a single heavy outer piece. Men can pack capris. Women can pack a light shawl. The patterned elephant pants and printed scarves sold at every market are useful both as souvenirs and as quick coverage. Local prices are not posted, so for reference (1 USD ≈ 35 THB):

- Elephant pants: 100 to 200 THB
- Shawls or scarves: 90 to 150 THB

To check garment quality on the spot: count the seams and look at how the edges are finished, feel the fabric for thickness and consistency, and check the zippers (zipper quality is a reliable proxy for overall quality).

**Get mobile data before you land.** You will want connectivity from the moment you exit the terminal, both for navigating and for calling a Grab. An eSIM through Airalo is the easiest path. They offer a 10-day, 50 GB plan or an unlimited 15-day plan for around $10 and $20 respectively. (Disclosure: my referral link gives both of us a $3 credit.)

## Bangkok

Bangkok is the entry point for almost every Thailand itinerary, and it rewards a few days even if your real interest is the islands or the north. The city is dense, hot, and well-served by transit, and it absorbs a first-time visitor more gently than you might expect.

### Logistics

Bangkok has a clean public transit system: the BTS Skytrain above ground, the MRT subway, and ferries on the Chao Phraya River. Use those plus Grab. If you take a metered taxi, make sure the driver actually starts the meter before you leave; flat-rate negotiations almost always lose to the meter.

Tipping is appreciated but not required at restaurants and hotels. Markets and tuk-tuks expect light haggling, but do it lightly. Cards work in restaurants and malls; markets and small shops want cash. Plan around traffic. Rush hours in Bangkok are slower than they look on a map, so build in extra time, or schedule the activity for an hour you would not normally pick.

### Where to stay

**Sukhumvit.** The cosmopolitan core, with modern hotels, malls, and nightlife. Easy access to the BTS, international food, and bars. The trade-off is noise and a less culturally distinct atmosphere than other neighborhoods.

**Khao San Road.** Backpacker territory. Hostels, street food, and cheap bars. Loud and party-oriented; not the choice if you are here for culture.

**Silom.** A mix of business and night markets, with rooftop bars and easy access to Lumpini Park. Fewer cultural attractions immediately at hand.

**Riverside.** The most upscale option, with views of the Chao Phraya and a calmer atmosphere. Close to Wat Arun and the river ferries. More expensive and a little farther from the action.

### What to do

The headline temples are Wat Phra Kaew and the Grand Palace (home of the Emerald Buddha), Wat Arun (the Temple of the Dawn, at its best from the opposite bank at sunset), and Wat Pho (the reclining Buddha and the affiliated massage school).

For markets, Chatuchak is the largest open-air market in the city and a good destination if you want to shop. The floating markets at Damnoen Saduak and Amphawa are touristy but visually distinct. Asiatique combines a night market with a riverside promenade.

The Chao Phraya river ferries are worth taking at least once. They run between most of the temple stops and are cheaper than tuk-tuks. For nightlife, Vertigo and Sky Bar are the well-known rooftop options. The clubs and bars in Sukhumvit and along RCA are the more energetic version. For food, work through Chinatown and the night markets, and consider a Thai cooking class if you have a half-day to spare.

### Weather in February and March

February and March are warm and dry. Daytime highs run 30 to 35°C; nights rarely drop below 25°C. Rain is uncommon, but humidity makes the heat feel heavier. Light clothing, sunscreen, and water are the basics; malls and temples are the indoor refuges during peak afternoon heat.

## Khao Yai

Khao Yai is the closest serious nature break from Bangkok. The national park is large, the surrounding region is dotted with vineyards, and a few days here is a good counter to the density of the city.

### Logistics

Khao Yai is about a three-hour drive from Bangkok. The simplest route is to rent a car or hire a private transfer. You can also take a train or bus to Pak Chong, the nearest town, and then move around with local taxis. To enter Khao Yai National Park you pay an entry fee (around 400 THB for foreigners). Distances inside the park are long; a car or scooter is needed to cover any meaningful ground. If you plan to hike, bring real shoes, insect repellent, and more water than feels necessary.

### Where to stay

**Near the park entrance.** Best if you intend to be in the park most days. Close to trailheads. Fewer dining options outside the resort itself.

**Pak Chong.** The most economical base, with more local services and easier transport access. A bit removed from the main attractions.

**Wine country.** Boutique hotels surrounded by vineyards. Quieter and more comfortable, but you will need a car or driver for everything.

### What to do

The park itself is the draw. Haew Narok is the tallest waterfall in the park; Haew Suwat appeared in the film *The Beach*. Morning and evening drives are the time to look for elephants, deer, gibbons, and birdlife. Trails range from short to multi-hour.

Outside the park, the area's wineries (PB Valley and GranMonte are two of the better-known ones) are worth a half-day for tastings and a hillside lunch. Chokchai Farm is an interactive dairy operation with horse rides and house-made ice cream. Palio Khao Yai is a small Italian-themed retail village useful for an hour or two of shopping and food. Pha Diao Dai is the standard panoramic viewpoint.

### Weather in February and March

Warm and dry. Daytime temperatures range from 20 to 32°C, with cool mornings and warm afternoons. Waterfalls flow less in this period because the rains are still months away, but the views are clearer and the air is generally good. Pack a light jacket for the mornings.

## Chiang Mai

Chiang Mai is quieter than Bangkok and easier to walk. A week here is a reasonable counterweight to a week in the south.

### Where to stay

**Old City.** Inside the moat, surrounded by temples and night markets. Close to the cultural center. Can get loud after dark.

**Nimmanhaemin.** The modern, design-forward neighborhood. Cafes, boutiques, and the digital nomad crowd. Less temple density, more traffic.

**Riverside.** Quieter, set along the Ping River. Hotels skew upscale. Farther from the Old City and you will need to commute.

**Doi Suthep.** Up the mountain. Closer to the famous temple and the nature, but you will need transport to get into the city.

### What to do

The temples to start with are Wat Phra That Doi Suthep (on the mountain, with the long climb up the staircase and the city view), Wat Chedi Luang (in the Old City), and Wat Phra Singh (notable for its Lanna-era architecture).

For nature, Elephant Nature Park is the credible ethical sanctuary in the area. Doi Inthanon is the highest point in Thailand, with trails and waterfalls. Sticky Waterfalls (Bua Tong) have a calcified surface you can climb without slipping.

Markets and food are the other reason to be here. The Sunday Walking Street is the busiest weekly market. Warorot is the more local everyday market. Try khao soi at least once; the curry noodle dish is the local specialty. If you want to slow down, take a Thai cooking class or join a meditation course at one of the temples. If you do not, settle into one of the Nimmanhaemin cafes for the afternoon and book a Thai massage.

### Weather in February and March

The transition between cool season and hot season. Mornings and evenings are mild (15 to 20°C); afternoons climb to 30 to 35°C. Late February into March can bring smoke from agricultural burning in the surrounding region, which lowers air quality and visibility. If you are visiting in March, plan more time in the higher-elevation areas where the air is clearer.

## Phuket

Phuket is the largest island in Thailand and the standard beach destination. It runs the full range from luxury resort coastline to crowded backpacker bars.

### Logistics

Phuket has its own international airport with direct flights from Bangkok (about an hour and a half), other Asian capitals, and increasingly from Europe and Australia. Flying from Bangkok is faster and not much more expensive than the overnight bus.

On the island, use Grab or taxis. Fares are higher than elsewhere in Thailand. Renting a scooter or small car is the practical way to see the island at your own pace. Tuk-tuks are common but expensive; negotiate firmly. Carry cash for markets, tuk-tuks, and small restaurants.

### Where to stay

**Patong Beach.** The center of nightlife. Bars, clubs, restaurants, large hotels. Loud and tourist-saturated; not the place for rest.

**Kata Beach.** A calmer alternative, popular with families and beginning surfers. Less nightlife, fewer crowds.

**Karon Beach.** A middle ground between Kata's calm and Patong's intensity. Wide beach, restaurants and shops without the bar density.

**Kamala Beach.** Quieter, with boutique hotels and resorts. Good for couples and families. Limited nightlife.

**Bang Tao Beach.** The high-end stretch. Upscale resorts including Laguna Phuket. Quiet, removed from the busy areas.

### What to do

For nature and water, Freedom Beach is a smaller hidden cove with clear water. The Phi Phi Islands are the standard day-trip destination for snorkeling and beaches; they are crowded, but the scenery holds up. James Bond Island is the kayaking trip, often combined with a day on the bay.

For culture, the Big Buddha is the large white statue on the hill with a panoramic view. Wat Chalong is the principal temple on the island. Old Phuket Town has surviving Sino-Portuguese architecture and a few good cafes and shops.

For everything else: ethical elephant sanctuaries (look up the operator carefully before booking), Promthep Cape at sunset, and night markets like Naka for food and souvenirs. If you are not in the mood to leave the resort, the beach clubs (Catch and Kudo are two of the well-known ones) cover the same territory with a cocktail.

### Weather in February and March

Dry season. Days run 28 to 34°C, nights around 24°C. Humidity is moderate, rain is rare, the sea is calm. The right window for beaches and snorkeling.

## Koh Samui

Koh Samui covers the same brief as Phuket (beaches, easy resort holiday) but is harder to reach and quieter on average. It is the right choice if you want to combine some Bangkok time with a beach week and you are willing to pay a small premium for the island flight.

### Logistics

Koh Samui has a small, open-air airport but no direct international flights. The standard route is a connection through Bangkok on Bangkok Airways, which holds the operating license for most Samui flights and prices accordingly. Buy anything you need for the week in Bangkok before you connect: prices on the island are higher and selection is thinner.

Transport on the island is more expensive than the mainland. Use Grab where you can; the airport hotel transfer is usually the easiest first leg. Taxis and motorbikes within the island run around 300 THB per trip. A scooter rental is the freedom option but requires a license and either a deposit or your passport held overnight.

### Where to stay

The island is roughly circular, with a single main road around the perimeter. Most accommodations sit along this road, on one of the beaches. Pick the beach for the vibe.

**Chaweng Beach.** The busy stretch, with shops, water sports, and restaurants. Close to the airport. Not the place for sunset views (it faces east).

**Lamai Beach.** Quieter than Chaweng but not isolated. The Renaissance Koh Samui sits here.

**Bophut Beach (Fisherman's Village).** Family-leaning, with a strong food scene. Less crowded.

**Choeng Mon Beach.** The quietest of the four. Boutique and high-end resorts. Almost no nightlife.

### What to do

If you want to move: Wat Phra Yai (the Big Buddha on the northeast point), Wat Plai Laem (the colorful temple with the eighteen-armed statue), Angthong Marine National Park (snorkeling, hiking, and 42 islands), the Secret Buddha Garden in the hills, and Namuang Waterfall. If you do not want to move, Samui rewards that too. The beach clubs, the resort pools, and the slow afternoons are the actual draw for most visitors.

### Weather in February and March

Dry season. Days are 30 to 32°C; nights rarely below 25°C. The sea is calm. Rain is uncommon. The combination of clear skies and warm water is the reason February and March are the popular months to be here.

## US visa requirements (for transit or visiting)

The United States has separate flows for tourism or business and for transit, and many travelers conflate them.

**Tourism or business.** The standard route is the B-1/B-2 visa. Citizens of countries in the Visa Waiver Program (most of Western Europe, Australia, Japan, and others) can travel under ESTA for stays up to 90 days, but ESTA must be approved before boarding. Other nationalities typically need an interview at a US embassy or consulate.

**Transit.** If you are connecting through a US airport on the way to a third country, you usually need either a C-1 transit visa or, if eligible, ESTA. The US does not have a sterile international transit zone, so even a same-day connection requires entering the country and clearing immigration.

**Important details.** ESTA approvals are typically valid for two years, but travel must be confirmed before each trip. Visa interviews can take weeks to months to schedule depending on the consulate. Check the official guidance on travel.state.gov before booking, and confirm the rules for your specific nationality.
