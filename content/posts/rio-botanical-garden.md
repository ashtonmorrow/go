The Jardim Botânico do Rio de Janeiro is one of the better-preserved 19th-century botanical gardens in Latin America, and one of the easiest places in the city to spend a half-day if you want a break from the beaches and the heat. I went on a partly-rained-out afternoon and left covered in mosquito bites, both of which I should have planned for. What follows is what I would tell someone going for the first time.

## The basic facts

The garden was founded in 1808 by Prince Regent Dom João (later King João VI of Portugal) shortly after the Portuguese royal family relocated to Rio to escape Napoleon. It opened to the public in 1822 and has been in continuous operation since. It sits in the Jardim Botânico neighborhood of Rio's South Zone, on flat land between the Lagoa Rodrigo de Freitas and the base of the Corcovado mountain.

A note on the location, because the source I drafted from had this wrong: the Botanical Garden is **not** in Quinta da Boa Vista. Quinta da Boa Vista is a different park in the North Zone, the former imperial residence and current site of the National Museum. The two are often confused in older travel writeups.

The total area is about 350 acres (140 hectares). Roughly 130 acres (54 hectares) are open to visitors. The collection holds about 6,500 species of plants, including a number of rare and endangered tropical species.

The garden is not the closest attraction to Christ the Redeemer. The Cristo statue sits on top of Corcovado, and while the mountain rises behind the garden, the actual access route to the statue runs from a different side. Plan them as separate visits.

## Getting there

There is no direct metro service to the garden. Travel writeups that point at "Line 1 to the Jardim Botânico station" are out of date or wrong. The practical options are:

- **Uber, 99, or taxi.** This is what I did. From most of the South Zone (Copacabana, Ipanema, Leblon, Botafogo) it is a 10 to 20 minute ride depending on traffic.
- **Bus.** Several lines stop near the entrance. Useful if you are already on a bus route in the area, less so if you are not.

## Tickets and pricing

Buy tickets online at jbrt.org.br before you go. On-site purchases are cash-only at the time of writing, which is the kind of detail that catches travelers who arrive expecting to tap a card.

Pricing is tiered by where you live:

## What to see

**Avenue of Royal Palms.** The signature feature: a long allée of tall palm trees planted in 1842, leading toward the central area. Worth walking even in the rain.

**Japanese Garden.** A self-contained section with Japanese maples, cherry blossoms, bonsai, a koi pond, and a bamboo grove. One of the older parts of the garden's expansion.

**Old Gunpowder Factory.** A small museum on the original gunpowder works that occupied the site before the garden absorbed it. The factory was part of the same 1808-era royal complex; it has been preserved as an exhibit on the early industrial history of imperial Rio.

**Cactus Garden.** A drier display of succulents and cacti from across the Americas. A useful contrast with the surrounding tropical foliage.

**Orchid Greenhouse.** Several hundred orchid species, including some rare and endangered ones. Concentrated in a single greenhouse, so you can see most of it in an hour.

## What I would have done differently

**Bring insect repellent.** The garden is humid and tropical and the mosquitoes treat it accordingly. I left covered in bites. If you are coming from a Northern Hemisphere city in winter, you have probably forgotten what that feels like.

**Watch the weather window.** Rio's afternoon thunderstorms in the rainy season (roughly November through March) come fast and dump hard. There is some shelter in the greenhouses and the Gunpowder Factory museum, but most of the garden is exposed. If the forecast is questionable, go in the morning.

**Two to three hours is enough for most visitors.** A botany enthusiast can spend longer. A casual visitor will see the headline sections in a single relaxed walk.
