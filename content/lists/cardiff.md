Cardiff is the Welsh capital you can cross on foot in half an hour: a castle in the middle, a regenerated bay, and a stadium that drops 70,000 rugby fans into the center on a match day. A day or two does it, alone or as the anchor for a wider Wales trip. Here's how I'd spend it.

## How many days do I need?

One or two days covers Cardiff comfortably. A single day handles the castle, the Victorian arcades, and the National Museum. A second day adds Cardiff Bay and a slower pace, or a trip out to St Fagans or Castell Coch.

The center is small and flat, and almost everything in this guide is within a twenty-minute walk of Cardiff Castle. The one thing to check before you book is the rugby calendar, covered below.

## How do I get to Cardiff?

**Great Western Railway trains** run from London Paddington to **Cardiff Central** in about 2 hours, direct and roughly twice an hour. Advance fares booked ahead are cheaper than walk-up. Cardiff Central station sits in the city center, a short walk from the castle and the arcades.

**Cardiff Airport (CWL)** is small, about 12 miles west of the city, with a limited set of routes. Many visitors instead fly into **Bristol Airport** across the Severn estuary, or into Birmingham, and continue by train.

A scheduling note worth knowing: on a **Wales rugby match day**, especially during the Six Nations from late January to mid-March, the **Principality Stadium** sits in the middle of the city and 70,000 people fill the center. Hotels book out and prices climb. Plan around the fixture list, or come for it deliberately, because match-day Cardiff is the city at full volume.

## Where should I stay?

Stay in the **city center** for a first visit. A central base puts the castle, the arcades, the station, and most of the restaurants on foot.

The streets around Cardiff Castle and St Mary Street are the convenient, lively core, close to everything and to the nightlife. **Cardiff Bay**, the regenerated waterfront about a mile and a half south, is the calmer, scenic alternative, with the Wales Millennium Centre and the Senedd, and a quick train or bus link to the center. ## The castle and the arcades

**Cardiff Castle** sits in the literal center of the city, a 2,000-year site that layers a Roman fort, a Norman keep on its motte, and an extravagant Victorian-Gothic mansion remodeled for the Marquess of Bute. The interiors are the surprise, lavishly decorated room by room. Allow a couple of hours.

The **Victorian and Edwardian arcades** are the most characterful part of the center, a network of covered shopping lanes lined with independent shops, record stores, and cafes. Among them, the covered **Cardiff Market** is the old indoor market hall, good for a cheap lunch. The **National Museum Cardiff**, near the civic buildings of Cathays Park, is free and holds a strong Impressionist collection alongside Welsh art and natural history.

Cardiff is a green city. **Bute Park**, the long landscaped park, runs north along the River Taff straight from the castle wall. [Crofters Garden](/pins/crofters-garden) is another of the city's pinned green spaces.

## Cardiff Bay

**Cardiff Bay** is the regenerated dockland south of the center, the old coal-exporting waterfront rebuilt around a freshwater bay. It is a flat, easy waterside walk.

The landmark is the **Wales Millennium Centre**, the bronze-fronted arts venue with a line of Welsh and English poetry cut into its facade. Beside it, the **Senedd** is the home of the Welsh Parliament, open to visitors. **Mermaid Quay** holds the bay's run of restaurants and bars, and the small red-brick **Norwegian Church**, where Roald Dahl was christened, sits along the water. A bay visit is an easy half-day from the center.

## Where should I eat?

Cardiff eats well for its size, and the food clusters in the center around the arcades and St Mary Street, with more restaurants out at Mermaid Quay in the bay.
