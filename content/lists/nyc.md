New York City is too big to finish, and the first-timer mistake is trying. It is 8.5 million people across five boroughs, and a first visit, four or five days, should be built around a couple of neighborhoods and a short list of sights. Here's how I'd choose.

## How many days do I need?

Four or five days is the shape of a first visit. That covers the major sights, two or three neighborhoods explored properly, a museum or two, and a day across the river in Brooklyn, without the forced-march pace that ruins a New York trip.

The city has years of things to do, so the planning is about choosing well, not covering everything. Pick a few neighborhoods, go deep, and let the rest wait for the next trip. Trying to tick off all five boroughs in a long weekend is how people come home tired and unconvinced.

## How do I get in from the airports?

Three airports serve the city, and the best one depends on where you land.

| Airport | Where it is | Into Manhattan |
|---|---|---|
| **JFK** | Queens, southeast | AirTrain to the subway or the LIRR commuter train. Flat-rate taxi about 70 dollars plus tolls and tip |
| **LaGuardia (LGA)** | Queens, closest to Manhattan | No direct train. The Q70 bus links to the subway. Taxi 40 to 60 dollars |
| **Newark (EWR)** | New Jersey, west | AirTrain to a NJ Transit train into Penn Station, around 15 dollars. Often the smoothest |

Rideshare works from all three but surge pricing and airport pickup fees can push it past the taxi rate. For JFK and Newark, the train is usually faster than a car in rush-hour traffic.

## Where should I stay?

Most first-time visitors stay in Manhattan, and the real rule is to stay near a subway line. The borough matters less than the walk to the train.

| Area | Why pick it | Trade-off |
|---|---|---|
| **Midtown** | The most central and best-connected, near Times Square, Grand Central, and most transit | Busy, expensive, and the least characterful part of the city |
| **Lower Manhattan** | FiDi and Tribeca, near the 9/11 Memorial and the ferries, quieter at night | A longer ride up to Midtown sights |
| **Village / SoHo / Chelsea** | Atmosphere, restaurants, and walkable streets, the most New York-feeling base | Among the priciest rooms in the city |
| **Brooklyn** | Williamsburg, Brooklyn Heights, and DUMBO, with skyline views and more space | A subway ride from Manhattan sights, though a short one |
| **Long Island City (Queens)** | Lower rates, fast subway to Midtown, a quick value base | Less to do at street level after dinner |

## How is the city laid out?

New York has five boroughs: **Manhattan**, **Brooklyn**, **Queens**, **the Bronx**, and **Staten Island**. A first trip is mostly Manhattan, with a day or two in Brooklyn.

**Manhattan** is the long, narrow island most of the famous sights sit on. Above 14th Street it runs on a numbered grid, which makes it easy to navigate: streets run east-west, avenues run north-south, and addresses climb as you go north. Below 14th Street, in Greenwich Village and the older downtown, the grid breaks and the streets have names, which is where visitors get briefly lost.

**Brooklyn**, across the East River, is the borough most worth a first-trip day, especially Williamsburg, DUMBO, and Brooklyn Heights. **Queens** is the most diverse, with the city's deepest range of food. **The Bronx** holds Yankee Stadium, the zoo, and the botanical garden. **Staten Island** is mostly residential, but the free Staten Island Ferry past the Statue of Liberty is one of the best free things in the city.

## The first-timer sights

The famous list is long. These are the ones a first trip is built around, with the booking advice that saves the queue.

- **Central Park** is the 843-acre park in the middle of Manhattan, free, and best entered with no fixed plan. Half a day minimum.
- **The Statue of Liberty and Ellis Island** are reached by ferry from Battery Park. Book the ferry online ahead, and book far ahead if you want crown access.
- **The observation decks** are a choice, not a checklist: the Empire State Building, Top of the Rock at Rockefeller Center, Edge in Hudson Yards, and Summit One Vanderbilt. Pick one, buy a timed ticket online.
- **The museums** that anchor a trip are the Metropolitan Museum of Art, the Museum of Modern Art, and the American Museum of Natural History. Book timed entry. The Met operates on a suggested-donation basis for New York State residents only.
- **The 9/11 Memorial and Museum** in Lower Manhattan. The outdoor memorial pools are free, and the museum is a timed ticket.
- **The High Line** is the elevated park built on a former rail line on the west side, free, best walked end to end.
- **The Brooklyn Bridge** walk from Manhattan to DUMBO is free and one of the best hours in the city. Go early to beat the crowds.

A note on Times Square: the costumed characters and the people handing out "free" CDs or bus tour tickets expect payment and can be pushy. Enjoy the lights, keep walking, and do not let anyone put a costume arm around you for a photo.

## How does the subway work?

The **New York City subway** runs 24 hours a day and reaches all five boroughs. It is faster than a taxi anywhere in Manhattan during the day.

Pay with **OMNY** by tapping a contactless credit card, debit card, or phone directly at the turnstile. The old MetroCard has been retired. The fare is a flat **2.90 dollars** per ride regardless of distance, and OMNY caps your spending after 12 rides in a 7-day window, so the rest of that week rides free. One tap covers free transfers between subway lines.

Two practical notes. Express trains skip stops, local trains make every stop, and the same line runs both, so check the train before you board. And service changes on nights and weekends are constant in New York, so check the posted notices or a transit app rather than trusting the map on the wall.

## Where should I eat?

New York's best eating is the range, not the price. The whole world cooks here, and a good food day moves between a hawker stall, a pizza slice, a deli counter, and a borough you have not tried. Queens in particular is worth a dedicated food day.
