[Ulm](/cities/ulm) is a city of about 130,000 people on the Danube in southwest Germany, on the fast rail line between Stuttgart and Munich. It is known for one extraordinary thing, the Minster with the tallest church steeple in the world, and for a pretty half-timbered Old Town along the river below it. A day covers Ulm comfortably. This guide covers getting in, where to stay, the Minster and the Old Town, the Fishermen's Quarter and the Danube, and where to eat.

## How long do I need?

One day covers Ulm well. That is enough for the Minster and the spire climb, the Old Town and the market square, and a walk through the Fishermen's Quarter along the Danube.

Ulm sits on the main line between Stuttgart and Munich, so it works best as a day stop or an overnight on a wider southern Germany trip. It is a strong half-day rather than a base for a long stay.

## How do I get to Ulm?

Ulm is on Germany's **ICE high-speed rail network**, on the Stuttgart-Munich line, so trains are frequent and fast. Stuttgart is about an hour away, Munich about an hour and a quarter. **Ulm Hauptbahnhof** is a short walk from the Minster and the Old Town.

The nearest airports are **Stuttgart** and **Munich**, with Memmingen as a smaller budget-airline option to the south. Inside Ulm itself you do not need transport, since the center is small and walkable.

## Where should I stay?

Stay in the center, near the Minster and the Old Town, since everything in this guide is within a short walk.

## The Minster and the Old Town

The **Ulm Minster**, the Ulmer Münster, is the city. Its steeple rises about 161.5 meters, the tallest of any church in the world, and the climb to the viewing platform is 768 steps up a narrowing stone staircase, with the upper stages open and exposed. On a clear day the view reaches south to the Alps. The Minster is a Protestant church, free to enter, and the tower climb is a separate paid ticket.

Around it the **Old Town** runs on a market square, the Marktplatz, fronted by the ornate Gothic Rathaus and, on the cathedral square, the modern white **Stadthaus** by the architect Richard Meier, a deliberate contrast. Near the station, a small memorial marks the birthplace of **Albert Einstein**, who was born in Ulm in 1879.

## The Fishermen's Quarter and the Danube

The **Fischerviertel**, the Fishermen's and Tanners' Quarter, is the prettiest part of Ulm, a cluster of half-timbered houses, narrow lanes, and small canals where the Blau stream runs into the Danube. The **Crooked House**, the Schiefes Haus, leans dramatically over the water and now operates as a hotel.

The **Danube** itself forms the city's southern edge and the historic border with Bavaria, and the town of Neu-Ulm sits directly across the river. A stretch of the old city wall survives along the bank, including the leaning **Metzgerturm**, the Butchers' Tower.

## Where to eat

Ulm eats on Swabian cooking, the regional style of *Maultaschen* filled pasta, *Spätzle*, and hearty meat plates, with a good spread of international kitchens on top.
