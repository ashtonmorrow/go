Benidorm is a beach resort on the Costa Blanca in southeast Spain, instantly recognizable for its wall of high-rises rising behind two long sandy beaches. It is one of Europe's busiest package-holiday destinations, especially with British visitors, and it is exactly what it sets out to be. A few days covers it. This guide covers getting in, where to stay, the beaches and the Old Town, and where to eat.

## How long do I need?

Benidorm is a beach resort rather than a sightseeing city, so the length of a trip depends on how much beach and resort time you want. The Old Town and the viewpoint take an hour or two, and the rest is sun, sea, and the evening scene.

Most visitors come for a beach week. A few days is the minimum that makes the trip worth it, and the season matters more than the day count.

## How do I get to Benidorm?

**Alicante-Elche Airport** is the nearest airport, about an hour away. From Alicante, the coastal **TRAM light rail** runs a scenic route along the Costa Blanca into Benidorm, and buses also connect the two.

A car is not needed for a Benidorm beach trip, since the resort is walkable and the TRAM covers the coast. A car helps only if you plan day trips up into the hills, such as the mountain village of Guadalest.

## Where should I stay?

Benidorm's accommodation is concentrated in the high-rise blocks behind the two beaches. The area behind **Levante Beach** is the livelier base, closer to the nightlife and entertainment. The area behind **Poniente Beach** is generally calmer and more family-oriented.

The **Old Town**, on the headland between the two, has smaller hotels and a more traditional feel. The atlas does not yet carry hotel pins for Benidorm, so book on the usual sites and read recent reviews for the property.

## The beaches and the Old Town

Benidorm runs on its two beaches. **Levante Beach** is the busier, livelier strip, backed by bars and the entertainment scene. **Poniente Beach**, on the other side of the headland, is the longer and generally calmer of the two. Both are wide, clean, and the reason the town exists.

Between them sits the small **Old Town**, the Casco Antiguo, the fishing village Benidorm grew from, now a knot of narrow streets, churches, and tapas bars. At its tip, the **Balcón del Mediterráneo** is the blue-and-white tiled viewpoint terrace looking out over the sea, the one classic photo stop in town.

## Where to eat

Benidorm eats on a wide resort range: Spanish tapas and seafood alongside a heavy presence of British and international food built for the package-holiday crowd. The Old Town has the more traditional Spanish kitchens.
