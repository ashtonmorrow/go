[Luang Prabang](/cities/luang-prabang) is a small town in northern Laos, set on a peninsula where the Nam Khan river meets the Mekong. Its old town is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, blending gilded Lao Buddhist temples with French colonial shophouses, and the whole place runs at a deliberately slow pace. A first visit is three or four days. This guide covers getting in, where to stay, the old town and temples, Kuang Si Falls, and where to eat.

## How long do I need?

Three or four days is the right amount of Luang Prabang. The old town and its temples take a day or two, and the rest goes to Kuang Si Falls, a slow river trip, and the unhurried rhythm the town is built around.

This is a place to settle into rather than tick off. Rushing it misses the point, and most visitors who give it time wish they had given it more.

## How do I get to Luang Prabang?

Luang Prabang has an **international airport** with regional connections across Southeast Asia. The **Laos-China high-speed railway** also stops here, linking the town with Vientiane and the Chinese border in a few hours, which has made it far easier to reach than it once was.

The classic slow route is the **two-day Mekong boat** down from Huay Xai on the Thai border, an overnight river journey rather than a means of transport. In town, the old quarter is small and walkable, and bicycles are easy to rent.

## Where should I stay?

Stay on or near the **peninsula**, the old town between the two rivers. From there the temples, the night market, and the riverfront are all on foot, and you wake up inside the part of town worth being in.

For a guesthouse in the heart of the old town, [Villa Chitdara](/pins/villa-chitdara) is the lodging pin on the Luang Prabang list. Beyond it the atlas does not yet carry a wide hotel pin set for the town, so book on the usual sites and read recent reviews for the property.

## The old town and the temples

The **old town** is the whole point of Luang Prabang, a low-rise peninsula of gilded monasteries, colonial shophouses, and cafes, small enough to cross on foot. **Wat Xieng Thong**, the 16th-century temple near the tip of the peninsula, is the standout, with its sweeping low roofs and a glass-mosaic tree of life. Saffron-robed monks are a constant presence, and the dawn **alms-giving**, the Tak Bat, is a daily religious ritual best watched quietly and from a distance, if at all.

**Mount Phousi**, the steep hill in the middle of town, is the climb for the sunset view over the rivers and the rooftops. At its foot, the former **Royal Palace** is now the national museum. As the day ends, the **night market** fills the main street with Lao textiles and handicrafts.

## Kuang Si Falls and around

**Kuang Si Falls**, the multi-tier waterfall about 30 km south of town, is the standout day trip. Its turquoise pools step down through the forest, several of them open for swimming, and a rescued-bear sanctuary sits near the entrance. Go early to beat the midday crowds, and allow a half-day with the travel.

The Mekong itself is the other half-day: slow boats run upriver to the **Pak Ou Caves**, two riverside caverns packed with thousands of old Buddha statues, often combined with a stop at a riverside village.

## Where to eat

Luang Prabang eats on Lao cooking, lighter and herb-forward, alongside a lasting French legacy of bakeries and cafes. Look for *khao soi* in its northern Lao form, river fish, sticky rice, and the bitter-herb stew *or lam*.
