[Marrakech](/cities/marrakesh) is a Moroccan city of about a million people at the foot of the High Atlas, built around a walled medieval medina that is a UNESCO World Heritage site. The part you came for sits inside those walls: the souks, the central square at Jemaa el-Fnaa, the palaces, and the riads tucked down lanes too narrow for a car. A first trip is three or four days, most of them in the old city, with one day saved for the mountains or the coast. This guide covers getting in, where to stay, the medina, the palaces and gardens, where to eat, the scams to expect, and the trips out of the city.

## How many days do I need?

Three or four days is the shape of a first trip. That covers Jemaa el-Fnaa by day and by night, a proper wander through the souks, the main palaces and gardens, and enough unhurried time in the medina that you stop counting sights and start reading the place. Two days works if Marrakech is one stop on a wider Morocco route through Fez and the coast.

The medina is dense, and the heat sets the pace from May through September, when afternoons climb past 100°F. Plan the walking for the morning and the early evening, and treat the middle of the day as courtyard-and-mint-tea time. In summer, pay up for a riad with a plunge pool.

## How do I get in from the airport?

**Marrakech Menara Airport (RAK)** sits about 6 km southwest of the medina, a 15-minute drive in normal traffic.

A **petit taxi** to Jemaa el-Fnaa should run roughly 70 to 100 dirham. The meters are rarely used for airport runs, so agree the fare before you get in rather than after. **Bus 19** runs an airport loop to Jemaa el-Fnaa and on through Gueliz for about 30 dirham, which is the cheap option if you are traveling light.

The easiest arrival is a **riad pickup**. Cars cannot reach most medina addresses, so the riad sends someone to meet you at a set drop point and walk you in through the lanes. For a first arrival, with luggage, in a maze you do not yet know, it is worth the small fee.

Morocco's **ONCF trains** connect Marrakech to Casablanca, Rabat, Tangier, and the airport-rail network if you are arriving overland. The station, Gare de Marrakech, is in Gueliz, a short taxi from the medina.

## Where to stay

The choice is a riad inside the medina or a hotel in the modern districts, and for a first trip the riad is the answer.

A **riad** is a traditional Moroccan courtyard house, built inward around a central patio or small garden, with the rooms looking onto the quiet middle rather than the street. Most medina riads are small, often under ten rooms, frequently restored with real care, and the courtyard does the work of shutting out the noise of the lanes. Staying in one is half of what the trip is. What you give up is real: you walk in and out through the souks, cars cannot reach the door, and the lanes are hard to learn for the first day.

**Gueliz** and **Hivernage** are the modern new-town districts west of the walls, laid out by the French with wide streets, larger hotels, pools, and easy taxis. They are calmer and more convenient, and they put you a ten-minute taxi from the old city. Pick them if a quiet, low-effort base matters more than atmosphere.

## The medina, the souks, and Jemaa el-Fnaa

[Jam3 El-Fna Maroc](/pins/jam3-el-fna-maroc), the great square known as Jemaa el-Fnaa, is the center of the medina and the place the city turns itself on. By day it is orange-juice carts, snake charmers, and water sellers. After dark it fills with food stalls, musicians, and storytellers, and the smoke and the crowd are the show. It is touristy and it is also the real heart of the city. Go in the evening and eat something.

[Koutoubia](/pins/koutoubia), the Koutoubia Mosque, stands just southwest of the square. Its 12th-century minaret is the tallest building in the medina and the landmark you navigate by when the lanes lose you. The mosque itself is closed to non-Muslims, but the gardens around it are open and the minaret is the orientation point of the whole old city.

The **souks** run north from Jemaa el-Fnaa, a covered warren of market lanes sorted loosely by trade. [Souk Semmarine](/pins/souk-semmarine) is the main artery, the wide entrance lane that feeds the smaller souks for leather, metal, lanterns, spices, and dyed wool. Getting lost is unavoidable and is the point. Prices are negotiable, the first number is high, and walking away is the strongest move you have.

Beyond the souks, the **Ben Youssef Madrasa** is the restored 16th-century Quranic school in the northern medina, one of the most beautiful interiors in the city, and **Le Jardin Secret** is a restored palace garden mid-medina that makes a good cool break from the lanes.

## Palaces and gardens

[Bahia Palace](/pins/bahia-palace) is the 19th-century palace in the southern medina, a sprawl of courtyards, painted cedar ceilings, and tiled rooms built for a grand vizier. It is the easiest palace to reach on foot from Jemaa el-Fnaa and the one to see if you only see one. Nearby, the **Saadian Tombs** are the 16th-century royal necropolis next to the Kasbah Mosque, sealed for centuries and reopened in 1917, small but worth the short queue.

Outside the walls, the **Jardin Majorelle** in Gueliz is the cobalt-blue garden created by the painter Jacques Majorelle and later owned by Yves Saint Laurent, now paired with the **YSL Museum** next door. It is the most-photographed spot in the city and tickets sell out, so book the timed entry online before you go.

## Common scams in the medina

Marrakech is broadly safe. The constant is the hustle, not danger, and a firm no handles nearly all of it.

**The faux guide.** A friendly local tells you the souk ahead is closed, or that there is a Berber market or a tannery "just today," and offers to walk you there. The destination is a shop that pays commission, and a fee will be expected at the end. Real licensed guides carry a badge. If you did not hire someone, you do not have a guide.

**The wrong-way redirect.** A version of the same move: someone insists the lane you are walking is closed or dangerous and points you another way, toward a shop or a dead end where a fee is requested for the "help." Trust your map and keep walking.

**The henna women.** In Jemaa el-Fnaa, women will reach for your hand and start applying henna before you have agreed to anything, then demand payment. Keep your hands to yourself and do not stop.

**The square performers.** The snake charmers, the monkey handlers, and the water sellers in Jemaa el-Fnaa expect payment for a photo, including a photo taken from a distance. If you do not want to pay, do not photograph them. A few coins settles it if you do.

**Taxi fares.** Petit taxi meters are often "broken" for tourists. Agree the fare before the ride, or ask the riad what the trip should cost.

None of this is aggressive, and none of it follows you far. The medina is worked by people making a living from visitors, and a steady walk with a clear no is the whole defense.

## Day trips from Marrakech

Marrakech sits at the foot of the High Atlas and three hours from the Atlantic, so a day out of the city changes the landscape completely. The [day trips from Marrakech](/cities/marrakesh/day-trips) page covers the High Atlas and Imlil, the walled port at Essaouira, the Ourika Valley, and Ouzoud Falls. All are long days, and the practical choice is a hired driver or a tour rather than piecing together public transport.
