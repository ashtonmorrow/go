[Seoul](/cities/seoul) is a ten-million-person capital that somehow feels easy, five-hundred-year-old palaces a few subway stops from glass towers and all-night markets, all knit together by a metro you can read from the first ride. A first trip is four or five days. Here's how I'd pick the neighborhoods.

## Getting in from the airport

**Incheon International Airport** is the main gateway, about 50 km west of the city. The **AREX**, the Airport Railroad Express, is the default way in. The all-stop train reaches Seoul Station in about an hour for a few thousand won. The non-stop Express service does the same run in about 43 minutes for more. Airport limousine buses run directly to specific hotel districts and are the easier choice with heavy luggage. **Gimpo Airport**, the second and closer airport, handles domestic and some regional international flights.

## Getting around

The **Seoul Metro** is the city. It is one of the most extensive and reliable subway systems in the world, fully signed and announced in English, and it reaches everything in this guide. Buy a **T-money card** at any convenience store or station machine, load it with cash, and tap onto every subway, bus, and most taxis. Trains stop around midnight, after which taxis are the fallback and are reasonably priced.

Many subway stations are landmarks in their own right because of the exit numbering. [Hongik University Station Exit 9](/pins/hongik-university-station-exit-9) is the example, the standard meeting point and entry into the Hongdae nightlife district.

## Where to stay

Pick a base by the kind of trip, and pick something on a metro hub.

| Where | Why pick it | Trade-off |
|---|---|---|
| Myeong-dong | Central, walkable, surrounded by shopping and street food, the best transit access | Touristy and busy. Not the most distinctive version of Seoul |
| Hongdae | Youth culture, live music, nightlife, the indie-and-art district | Loud at night. Better for a younger, later trip |
| Jongno / Insa-dong | Next to the palaces and the hanok quarters, calmer, the traditional-Seoul base | Quieter at night, fewer late-dinner options |
| Gangnam / COEX | Modern, polished, business-district comfort south of the river | Further from the historic core. A longer metro ride to the palaces |

For the Gangnam-side base, [InterContinental Seoul COEX](/pins/intercontinental-seoul-coex) sits on the COEX mall-and-convention complex, directly connected to the metro and the aquarium.

## The palaces and the old city

The historic core of Seoul sits north of the Cheonggyecheon stream, around the old Joseon palaces.

[Gyeongbokgung Palace](/pins/gyeongbokgung-palace) is the largest and the main one, the seat of the Joseon dynasty, with a changing-of-the-guard ceremony at the gate through the day. Changdeokgung, a short walk east, is the other major palace and a UNESCO site for its Secret Garden, worth pairing if palaces are the reason you came. [Gwanghwamun Square](/pins/gwanghwamun-square) is the wide ceremonial plaza in front of Gyeongbokgung, anchored by the statues of Admiral Yi Sun-sin and King Sejong.

[Bukchon Hanok Village](/pins/bukchon-hanok-village) is the hillside quarter of preserved hanok, traditional tiled-roof houses, threaded by lanes between the two main palaces. It is residential, so go early and quiet. [Insa-dong](/pins/insa-dong) is the neighboring street of galleries, tea houses, antiques, and craft shops, the easiest place to slow down. [Jogyesa](/pins/jogyesa) is the central Seoul temple of Korean Buddhism, a working temple in the middle of the city and the heart of the lantern festival each spring.

For something more offbeat, [Ihwa Mural Village](/pins/ihwa-mural-village) is the hillside neighborhood turned open-air art project on the slope of Naksan, and [Seodaemun Prison History Hall](/pins/seodaemun-prison-history-hall) is the preserved colonial-era prison, now a sober museum of the Japanese occupation and the Korean independence movement.

## Views, parks, and the river

[N Seoul Tower](/pins/n-seoul-tower) on Namsan is the headline viewpoint, reached by cable car or a walk up through the park, with the whole city laid out below. For a view earned on foot, [Inwangsan](/pins/inwangsan) is the granite peak on the city's northwest edge, a real but manageable climb past shamanist shrines and a stretch of the old city wall.

At ground level, [Cheonggyecheon](/pins/cheonggyecheon) is the restored stream that runs sunken through downtown, a long linear walk below the traffic. [Hangang Park](/pins/hangang-park) is the riverside parkland along the Han, the city's open-air living room, best in the evening when families and friends spread out with delivery fried chicken. [Seoul Forest](/pins/seoul-forest) is the larger green park east of the center, the calm half-day.

For families, [Coex Aquarium](/pins/coex-aquarium) sits inside the COEX mall in Gangnam. For photographers, [Yongma Land](/pins/yongma-land) is a small abandoned amusement park on the city's edge, a cult photo spot that opens to visitors for a small fee.

## Markets and where to eat

Seoul eats late and eats in markets, and the markets are the best food planning you can do.

[Gwangjang Market](/pins/gwangjang-market) is the headline, one of the oldest markets in the country and a dense hall of food stalls, the place for bindaetteok (mung-bean pancake), mayak gimbap, and raw beef. [Noryangjin Fisheries Wholesale Market](/pins/noryangjin-fisheries-wholesale-market) is the vast seafood market, where you buy from the stalls downstairs and have it cooked at a restaurant upstairs. [Myeong-dong](/pins/myeong-dong) runs a famous evening street-food strip alongside its shopping, the easiest introduction to Korean street snacks.

For a different register, [Common Ground](/pins/common-ground) in Konkuk is a retail-and-food complex built from stacked blue shipping containers, the photogenic modern counterpoint to the old markets.

## Festivals and big annual events

Seoul's calendar runs on the Korean lunar holidays plus a strong run of spring and autumn festivals. Two of the lunar holidays are worth planning around, since the city changes character.

| Event | When | What it changes |
|---|---|---|
| **Lotus Lantern Festival (Yeondeunghoe)** | Around Buddha's Birthday, in May | A UNESCO-listed festival, with thousands of paper lanterns and a vast lantern parade through Jongno. Jogyesa temple is the center of it. One of the best times to be in the city |
| **Cherry blossom season** | Early April, about 10 days | The blossoms peak along the Yeouido riverfront and the palace grounds. The Yeouido Spring Flower Festival is the focal point. Hotels climb in price for the window |
| **Seoul Lantern Festival** | November | Illuminated lantern installations strung the length of the Cheonggyecheon stream downtown. The autumn counterpart to the spring lanterns |
| **Seoul International Fireworks Festival** | Early October | A large fireworks display over the Han River, watched from Yeouido and the Hangang parks. Hundreds of thousands turn out |
| **Chuseok (Korean autumn harvest holiday)** | September or October, around three days | Korea's major family holiday. Seoul empties as residents travel to hometowns, and many shops and restaurants close. A strange, quiet time to visit, easier if you plan around the closures |
| **Seollal (Lunar New Year)** | Late January or February, around three days | The other big family holiday, with the same pattern. The city is quiet and many businesses close. The palaces often run special free-admission days |

The trip-shaping windows are the Lotus Lantern Festival in May and cherry-blossom season in early April, both of which are reasons to time a trip. Chuseok and Seollal are the windows to plan around rather than for, since the city's normal rhythm pauses.
