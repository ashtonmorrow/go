Mexico City is one of the great food cities on earth, and far easier and more rewarding than its reputation suggests. It is huge, it sits at 2,240 meters in a ring of mountains, and a first trip wants four or five days across a couple of neighborhoods. Here's how I'd plan it.

## How many days do I need?

Four or five days is the shape of a first visit. That covers the historic center and the Zócalo, a day for the Anthropology Museum and Chapultepec, a day in Coyoacán for the Frida Kahlo house, a full day out at Teotihuacán, and enough unhurried time to eat properly.

The city is enormous, and trying to cross it constantly will eat the trip. Pick neighborhoods that are close together, use Uber for the longer hops, and accept that a first trip leaves plenty for a second.

## How do I get in from the airport?

Mexico City has **two airports**, and the first thing to check when you book is which one your flight uses.

**Benito Juárez International (AICM)** is the main airport, just east of the city center, close in and easy. Buy a ticket for an **authorized taxi** at the official desk inside the terminal rather than accepting an offer in the hall, or take an **Uber**, which is reliable here and usually cheaper than the taxi.

**Felipe Ángeles International (AIFA)** is the newer airport, well north of the city, and the transfer into the center takes over an hour. Some budget and regional flights use it, so do not assume you are landing at the close-in airport.

## Where should I stay?

For a first visit, base in **Roma** or **Condesa**. These two adjoining neighborhoods are leafy, walkable, full of cafes and restaurants, and central enough that the historic core and the museums are a short ride away.

| Area | Why pick it | Trade-off |
|---|---|---|
| **Roma** | Walkable, full of restaurants and cafes, central, the most natural first-trip base | Popular and rising in price, busy on weekends |
| **Condesa** | Greener and calmer than Roma, built around two parks, still very walkable | Slightly more residential, fewer big sights nearby |
| **Polanco** | The upscale district, designer shopping, the Anthropology Museum, the best fine dining | Pricier and more corporate, less street life |
| **Centro Histórico** | In the middle of the major sights, grand old buildings, the most historic base | Quieter and emptier at night once the day crowds leave |

## The neighborhoods

Mexico City is best understood as a set of distinct neighborhoods rather than one center.

**Roma and Condesa** are the cafe-and-restaurant heart of a modern visit, side by side and easy to walk between. **Polanco** is the moneyed district, with Avenida Presidente Masaryk for shopping and a cluster of top restaurants. The **Centro Histórico** is the old colonial core around the Zócalo, the vast main square, and holds most of the headline monuments.

**Coyoacán**, in the south, is the cobblestoned former village where Frida Kahlo lived, with a calm plaza, markets, and the Casa Azul. **Chapultepec** is the giant park on the west side, holding the castle, the Anthropology Museum, and the zoo. Further south, **Xochimilco** is the network of canals where you ride a painted *trajinera* boat, a half-day in itself.

## What should I see?

The famous list is long. These are the anchors of a first trip.

- The **Zócalo**, the enormous main square, fronted by the **Catedral Metropolitana** and the **Palacio Nacional** with its Diego Rivera murals.
- **Templo Mayor**, the excavated ruins of the main Aztec temple, right beside the cathedral.
- The **Palacio de Bellas Artes**, the white marble cultural palace, with murals inside by Rivera, Orozco, and Siqueiros. [Hemiciclo a Juárez](/pins/hemiciclo-a-juarez), the white semicircular monument to Benito Juárez, stands across in the Alameda Central park.
- The **Museo Nacional de Antropología** in Chapultepec, the national archaeology museum and one of the best museums of its kind anywhere. Give it half a day.
- **Chapultepec Castle**, the hilltop castle and former imperial residence, with city views.
- The **Frida Kahlo Museum**, the Casa Azul in Coyoacán, where she was born and died. It sells timed tickets online and they sell out, so book ahead.
- **Teotihuacán**, the vast pre-Aztec pyramid complex about an hour northeast of the city, with the Pyramid of the Sun and the Pyramid of the Moon. The standard full-day trip.

## Is Mexico City safe?

Mexico City is broadly safe for a normal visit, and the central neighborhoods see millions of visitors a year without trouble. The reputation is worse than the day-to-day reality.

The sensible rules are the ones you would use in any large city. Use **Uber or registered taxis** rather than hailing a cab on the street. Do not flash a phone, a camera, or jewelry in crowds. Stick to well-traveled streets after dark and stay in the central districts at night. Drink bottled or filtered water. Beyond that, the central areas are walkable, friendly, and easy.

## Where should I eat?

Mexico City is one of the best eating cities in the world, and the range is the point: street tacos, neighborhood markets, century-old cantinas, and a deep modern restaurant scene. The best meals are rarely the most expensive ones. Start with **tacos al pastor**, the spit-roasted pork that is the city's signature, and work outward.
