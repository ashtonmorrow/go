[Zagreb](/cities/zagreb) is the capital of Croatia, a city of about 800,000 people set inland, away from the coast that draws most of the country's visitors. It is a relaxed, walkable Central European capital with a medieval hilltop old town, a famous open-air market, and a coffee culture that is a local institution in its own right. A day or two covers it. This guide covers getting in, where to stay, the Upper and Lower Towns, the market and cafe scene, and where to eat.

## How long do I need?

One full day covers the core: the Upper Town, Ban Jelačić Square, and the Dolac market. A second day adds the museums, the parks, and the slower pace that suits the city best.

Many travelers give Zagreb only a transit night between the airport and the coast. It is worth more than that. The city is a lived-in capital rather than a tourist set piece, and a day or two is the easy, low-pressure shape.

## How do I get into Zagreb?

Zagreb's **Franjo Tuđman Airport** sits about 17 km southeast of the center. A regular **airport shuttle bus** runs to the main bus station, from where the center is a short tram ride or walk. Taxis and rideshare take roughly 25 minutes.

Inside the city, an extensive tram network covers the Lower Town, and the historic core is small enough to walk. Zagreb is also a rail and bus hub, with connections to the coast and to neighboring capitals.

## Where should I stay?

Stay in or near the **Lower Town**, the 19th-century grid around Ban Jelačić Square. From there the Upper Town, the market, the museums, and the cafes are all on foot, and the trams cover anything further.

The streets around the main square and toward the train station are the convenient central choice. The atlas does not yet carry hotel pins for Zagreb, so book on the usual sites and read recent reviews for the property.

## The Upper Town and the Lower Town

Zagreb is two towns stacked together. The **Upper Town**, Gornji Grad, is the medieval core on the hill, holding **St. Mark's Church** with its bright tiled-roof coat of arms, the **Lotrščak Tower** with its noon cannon, the **Stone Gate** shrine, and a cluster of small museums, including the much-loved Museum of Broken Relationships.

The **Lower Town**, Donji Grad, spreads below it, a 19th-century grid of grand Austro-Hungarian buildings, museums, and a chain of green squares known as the Green Horseshoe. The short **funicular**, one of the steepest and shortest in the world, links the two, though the walk up is also quick.

## Dolac and the cafe culture

**Dolac**, the open-air market just above Ban Jelačić Square, is the city's larder, a terrace of red-parasol stalls selling produce, flowers, cheese, and meat, with a covered hall below. Go in the morning. [Pod Zidom Bistro](/pins/pod-zidom-bistro) sits right at the market and cooks from it.

The other Zagreb institution is **coffee**. The weekend *špica*, the long mid-morning sit on a cafe terrace to see and be seen, is a genuine local ritual. **Tkalčićeva street**, a pedestrian run of cafes and bars climbing toward the Upper Town, is the densest place to join it.

## Where to eat

Zagreb eats on continental Croatian cooking, heartier than the coastal seafood style, with strong Central European and a growing international range on top.
