  - q: Is Cairo a good first trip outside the usual European or American comfort zone?
    a: Honestly, no. Cairo is one of the more demanding cities I have travelled in, and the hassle factor is real. If you have done other developing-world trips (Mumbai, Marrakech, Hanoi, Lima, parts of Bangkok) and managed the constant low-level sales pressure without it spoiling the trip, Cairo will be manageable. If your prior international travel is Western Europe and the Caribbean, the jump to Cairo is real and a guided trip is worth the money. The pyramids and the Grand Egyptian Museum are spectacular enough to earn the visit regardless; the question is how much friction you want between the airport and those experiences.
  - q: Should I hire a private guide?
    a: For the pyramid day, yes, unless you are a confident solo traveller who is fine pushing back on aggressive sales pitches twenty times an hour. A guide costs $80 to $150 USD for a full day (private car, English-speaking driver, English-speaking Egyptologist guide separate or combined) and saves you the entire scam-management layer on the plateau. For the rest of the city (Egyptian Museum or Grand Egyptian Museum, Islamic Cairo, Coptic Cairo) you can manage solo with Uber and an audio guide, but a half-day guided is still a low-friction way to get through a lot of history in a short trip.
  - q: Where should I stay for a first Cairo trip?
    a: Two answers. For the airport-arrival night, Le Méridien Cairo Airport, which is connected to the terminal by a jetbridge and saves you the late-night taxi negotiation. For the actual trip, either the Giza / 6th of October side (Hyatt Centric West Cairo and the rest of the corridor; closer to the pyramids, less central traffic) or the Zamalek / Garden City island hotels (more central, more traffic and noise, more of the city's evening life at your door). Pick by which experience you want; both work.
  - q: How do I handle Uber drivers asking for cash and more money than the app says?
    a: It happens on most rides in Cairo. The driver accepts the trip on the app, then either says the credit card payment is broken and asks for cash, or accepts the fare and then claims at the destination that traffic or distance made the price higher than what the app shows. Two ways to handle it. First, set up the Uber payment method as cash before you leave the hotel; have the exact app-quoted fare in small Egyptian pounds ready, and pay that amount only. Second, take a screenshot of the in-app fare at the start of the ride; if the driver claims a higher number at the end, show the screenshot and the app-fare, leave the agreed amount, and end the conversation. Tipping a few extra pounds for a longer ride is fine if the driver was helpful; agreeing to a 30 to 50 percent markup is not.
  - q: What are the common pyramid scams I should know going in?
    a: The five that will show up on a single visit. **The camel scam**, where someone offers a cheap or "free" camel ride for a photo, then demands a much higher fare to let you back off the camel; do not get on a camel without a written-down price agreed in advance. **The "free gift" scam**, where someone hands you a small carved scarab or a postcard "as a welcome", then loudly demands payment when you accept it; do not accept anything physical from anyone on the plateau. **The fake guide scam**, where someone in official-looking clothing offers to show you a "secret" view or "hidden" entrance, walks with you for ten minutes, then demands a tip; the only people authorised inside the site are the ones at the official ticket kiosks. **The ticket-resell scam**, where someone outside the gate offers to "skip the line" by selling you a ticket; the line is rarely long and the tickets they sell are either fake or stolen. **The taxi-back-to-the-city scam**, where the driver who brought you out offers to wait and take you back at "the same price" then triples it; use a fresh Uber for the return.
  - q: What is the dress code for the city and the sites?
    a: Cairo is more conservative than most beach-destination Egyptian visitors expect, and dressing accordingly reduces hassle from touts and reads more respectfully at religious sites. For men, long trousers (not shorts) and a t-shirt are fine in most contexts; long sleeves for mosques. For women, knees and shoulders covered for general city wear; mosques and churches need a scarf for the head, and long trousers or a long skirt. The Hyatt Centric and similar international hotels are Western-norm at the pool; the dress code is for the streets and the sites.

[Cairo](/cities/cairo) is one of the most demanding cities I have travelled in. Twenty-two million people in the metropolitan area, traffic that runs at all hours, and a constant low-level hustle around any visitor-facing surface. Touts at the pyramids, drivers angling for cash on Uber rides, the occasional friendly stranger whose welcome ends in a sales pitch. It is not a relaxing trip. The pyramids are worth every bit of the hassle; the Grand Egyptian Museum is the most impressive museum opening in a generation; the food is good and cheap; the history is genuinely staggering. The city rewards a traveller who shows up prepared.

This is the version of the trip I would book for someone who has done a few harder destinations before. If you are travelling internationally for the first time, this is not the city to learn on; a guided trip with a private driver and an Egyptologist for the pyramid days will absorb most of the friction and is worth the money. These notes assume you have decided to do the trip.

These are working notes from real time on the ground rather than a checklist. Cairo rewards going slowly more than it rewards covering ground, but the friction makes a long slow morning at one site beat a rushed half-day at three. Take what is useful, skip the rest.

## Getting in from the airport

Cairo International (CAI) sits about 22 km north-east of central Cairo and 30 km north-east of Giza. The arrival can be the worst part of the trip if you let it. Touts, taxi drivers, and tour operators line the arrivals hall the moment you clear customs, and Uber drivers will frequently take the trip on the app then renegotiate in cash when you get in the car. The single best decision you can make for a late or red-eye landing is to skip the city transfer that night entirely.

**The Le Méridien airport overnight is the single best Cairo-arrival decision** I can recommend. It connects directly to Terminal 3 by a jetbridge, so the path from "passport stamped" to "in your room" is a five-minute indoor walk with luggage on a normal floor (no curb, no taxi line, no shouted prices). The pool and bar area are decent, the breakfast is good, and Marriott platinum upgrades land cleanly. I showed up on a late first day and this hotel was the difference between a brutal first night and an actual night of sleep.

For arrivals at a normal hour with the energy for a transfer, Uber straight to your city hotel is the right default. Have the app open while you walk out, have small Egyptian pounds in hand, and treat the cash-push as the standard negotiation rather than a personal slight.

## Where to stay

The choice of base in Cairo is between the city-centre island neighbourhoods and the Giza / 6th of October corridor near the pyramids. Both work; the trade-off is whether you want to be inside the city's energy or just outside it.

My pick for a first Cairo visit centred on the pyramids is the Hyatt Centric in Giza. The trade-off (further from central Cairo's evening life) is the right one for a short trip; the morning to the plateau is fifteen minutes by Uber instead of an hour through traffic, and the late afternoon you come back to a pool and a bar that are not the city itself. For a longer Cairo stay or a return visit, Zamalek or Garden City unlocks the rest of the city and is worth the traffic.

## Getting around: Uber and the cash hustle

Uber works across all of Cairo and Giza and is the right default ride choice. Two things to know before your first ride. The first is that Cairo Uber drivers operate in a hybrid card-and-cash market that is unlike how Uber works in the US or Europe; the second is that the friction is the system, not the individual driver. A few practical rules make every ride cleaner.

**Set the Uber payment method to cash before your first Cairo ride.** The app accepts the fare, you pay the driver in Egyptian pounds in small notes at the destination. Card payment "works" on paper but a meaningful fraction of drivers will tell you the card system is down once you are in the car and ask for cash anyway; setting cash as the default skips that negotiation.

**Carry the right cash.** Have the app-quoted fare in small bills before you get in. 50 EGP and 100 EGP notes are the workable denominations; trying to pay a 200 EGP ride with a 500 EGP note that the driver "cannot break" is a frequent renegotiation moment.

**Screenshot the in-app fare at the start of the ride.** If the driver tries to add a "traffic surcharge" or quotes a higher number at the destination, the screenshot is the gentle way to end the conversation. Pay the app fare, add a small tip if the driver was helpful, leave.

**For longer plateau or museum days, hire a driver for the day.** Most hotels can arrange a private car-and-driver for 8 to 12 hours for $50 to $90 USD. You skip the per-ride negotiation entirely and the driver waits while you tour. This is what I would book for the Giza day, both for the friction-management and for the hands-free arrival back at the hotel.

The Cairo Metro exists and is cheap (a few EGP per ride) but is not really a visitor's tool; it does not reach Giza, the few lines are crowded, and the signage is patchy in English. Skip it for the trip unless you are a public-transit completionist.

## The pyramids: a DIY plan that works

The Giza Plateau has two entrances. The lower entrance is at the Sphinx end near the Mena House, immediately adjacent to the old village of Nazlet El-Semman; this is where every guided tour and most independent visitors start the day, and it is the most intense sales-pitch zone on the entire plateau. The upper entrance on Fayoum Road / the Desert Road sits at the top of the plateau, near the Pyramid of Khufu, and is the quieter one. The DIY play is to arrive at the upper gate fifteen minutes before opening, buy your ticket, and walk downhill through the site to the Sphinx.

**The plan:**

1. Open Uber from the Hyatt Centric or wherever you are based; set the destination as **"Pyramids upper gate"** or **"Fayoum Road entrance"**. If the driver gestures toward the lower gate, repeat "Fayoum Road" until they understand; some drivers will assume you want the Sphinx entrance because that is where most tourists are dropped.
2. Arrive about 10 to 15 minutes before the published opening time (currently 7:00 AM most of the year, 6:00 AM in summer). The kiosk at the upper gate sells tickets the same as the lower gate; the general site ticket is around 540 EGP for adults (about $11 USD at current rates). Inner-pyramid tickets for [the Pyramid of Khufu](/pins/pyramids-of-egypt), Khafre, or [Menkaure](/pins/pyramid-of-menkaure) are sold separately and are worth it for one if you want the experience of being inside a pyramid.
3. Walk down through the site. The path takes you past the three main pyramids on the way down toward [the Sphinx](/pins/great-sphinx-of-giza), with smaller queens' pyramids and the workers' cemeteries off to either side. The [Tomb of Meresankh III](/pins/tomb-of-meresankh-iii), an underrated rock-cut mastaba with original statues and painted reliefs, sits a short detour off the main path and is one of my favourite stops on the plateau because almost no one goes inside.
4. Finish at the Sphinx end. From there, walk out the lower gate to your prearranged driver or call a fresh Uber from the parking area. Do not let the driver who dropped you wait for the day; the "I'll take you back" return-trip price is consistently three times the app fare.

**Hours and water.** Plan for two to three hours on the plateau if you are not doing inner-pyramid tickets, four to five if you are. The site is in open desert with no shade once you are past the gates; bring water (no fountains inside), a hat, sun protection in any month, and a small bag with the essentials. Avoid going in the middle of the day in summer; the heat plus the hassle is a serious combination.

If you would rather not handle the negotiation layer at all, a private guide for the pyramid day runs $80 to $150 USD and is worth every dollar. You arrive at the upper gate in their car, they manage the kiosk, they walk you through the site explaining what you are looking at, they shield you from the constant approach, and they drop you back at your hotel. For most travellers' first pyramid day, this is the right answer.

## Common scams to know going in

The pyramid plateau is the densest scam environment I have encountered as a traveller, and the way to enjoy the visit is to know the patterns going in so you can pleasantly say no without breaking your stride. None of these are unique to Cairo, but the volume is.

- **The camel scam.** Someone offers a camel ride for what sounds like a friendly price ("only 50 pounds for a photo", "free, just to ride") and helps you onto the camel. Once you are on the animal, the price to get back down becomes very different: 500 EGP, 1,000 EGP, "the standard rate". Never get on a camel without a written-down price for the full ride agreed in advance, and never give the camel handler any money until you are back on the ground. The right answer is usually to skip the camel entirely; the photographs on a horse from a registered hotel stables back in Giza are better-tempered animals and a known fee.
- **The "free gift" scam.** Someone friendly approaches with a small carved scarab, a piece of papyrus, a postcard. They press it into your hand "as a welcome to Egypt". The moment you accept, the conversation pivots to payment, loud enough that the social pressure is the whole leverage. Do not accept anything physical from anyone on the plateau. A polite "no thank you" and keep walking is the answer; do not slow down to discuss.
- **The fake guide scam.** Someone in an official-looking shirt or a uniform-adjacent jacket offers to show you a "secret" view, a "hidden" tomb entrance, the "best photo spot". After ten minutes of walking they demand a tip; the more you have followed them, the more they ask for. The only official staff at the plateau are at the ticket kiosks and inside the inner-pyramid entries. Anyone outside those points who is offering guidance is freelance.
- **The ticket-resell scam.** Someone outside the gate offers to sell you a ticket "to skip the line". The line is rarely long enough to need skipping; the tickets they sell are either fake (you cannot enter) or stolen (you can enter, but the resale is illegal and the queue is short enough that the price difference makes no sense). Buy at the kiosk, always.
- **The return-trip taxi scam.** The driver who brought you out asks to wait and take you back at "the same price". After you have seen the pyramids the price triples on the return, and you have no leverage because they are the car. Pay the outbound fare in full at drop-off, get a fresh Uber for the return. Or, the cleaner version, book the private car-and-driver for the full day so the return is built in.

The honest framing on all of these: the people doing them are working a tourism economy that the wider Cairo economy does not fully provide for, and the scams are the friction that emerges. None of this is personal, none of it makes the city or the country worse than it is, and none of it changes how worthwhile the pyramids are once you are inside. Knowing the patterns going in is what makes the visit pleasant.

## Beyond Giza: the museum, the citadel, Coptic Cairo

Three other half-day stops earn the trip.

**The [Grand Egyptian Museum](/pins/grand-egyptian-museum-garden) (GEM)** opened the full collection in 2024 and is the new headline museum stop. The entire Tutankhamun trove (5,000+ artifacts; everything that was found in the 1922 tomb) is now displayed in one place for the first time in history; the grand staircase, the atrium, and the design of the building make this a genuinely world-class museum experience. Allow four to five hours. The [Bittersweet GEM café](/pins/bittersweet-gem) inside the museum is a fair lunch stop. Tickets are timed; book online ahead of your visit to skip the entry queue.

**The [Saladin Citadel](/pins/saladin-citadel)** is the 12th-century fortress on Muqattam Hill above central Cairo, with the Mosque of Muhammad Ali (the alabaster mosque) at its centre and the view over the city from the ramparts. Half a day with the [National Police Museum](/pins/national-police-museum), the smaller Sulayman Pasha mosque, and the view. Wear modest dress for the mosque visit; shoes off at the entrance.

**[Coptic Cairo](/pins/saints-sergius-and-bacchus-church)** (Old Cairo) is the cluster of early-Christian churches at the southern edge of the medieval city, including the Hanging Church, the Saint Sergius and Bacchus Church (where the Holy Family is said to have rested during their flight to Egypt), and the Coptic Museum. A quieter, slower visit than the pyramids; two hours covers the cluster on foot.

If you have an extra day, the [Heliopolis](/pins/el-korba-square) neighbourhood with its colonial-era Belle Époque architecture and the [Baron Empain Palace](/pins/baron-palace-garden) is the smaller-scale aside that most visitors never reach.

## When to go

Cairo is hot. The version of the trip that works best for almost everyone is the **November-to-March window**, when daytime temperatures sit in the 18 to 25°C range and the desert visits are comfortable. December and January are the busiest weeks because they coincide with US and European winter holidays; reservations at the pyramids' inner-tomb tickets and at GEM tighten up.

**April-May and October** are the shoulder months. Warmer (25 to 32°C), still manageable on the plateau if you go early, fewer crowds, hotel rates softer.

**June through September** is hot enough that the plateau is uncomfortable from 10:00 onwards; daytime temperatures regularly clear 35°C and can hit 40°C. If you can only travel in this window, book accommodation with a real pool, plan the pyramid day for sunrise-to-9:00, and spend the middle of the day indoors at GEM or the older Egyptian Museum.

The other consideration is **Ramadan**, which moves through the calendar; in 2026 it falls roughly mid-February to mid-March. Daytime food and drink service is reduced in many places, opening hours shift, and the evening Iftar break empties the streets briefly. It is a fascinating time to be in the city if you are aware of the rhythm, less fun if you have not factored it in.
