[Valencia](/cities/valencia) is Spain's easygoing third city, a walkable old town, a futuristic arts complex, and a former riverbed turned into a park that loops the whole center. It is the birthplace of paella. Two or three days. Here's how I'd spend them.

## How many days do I need?

Two or three days covers Valencia. That is enough for the old town and its markets, the City of Arts and Sciences, a walk or cycle through the Turia gardens, and time for the beach and a proper paella lunch.

Valencia is relaxed and walkable, and it does not need to be rushed. It also pairs well with the smaller Costa Blanca towns to the south for a longer trip.

## How do I get into Valencia?

**Valencia Airport** sits just west of the city and connects to the center by **metro** in about 20 minutes. Valencia is also on Spain's high-speed rail network, with **AVE trains from Madrid** in around an hour and three quarters.

Within the city, the metro, trams, buses, and a good bike network cover everything, and the old town itself is walkable.

## Where should I stay?

The **old town**, Ciutat Vella, is the best base for a first visit, putting the cathedral, the markets, and the restaurants on foot. **Ruzafa**, the neighborhood just south, is the trendier base, full of bars and restaurants.

If the beach is a priority, staying near the **Malvarrosa** puts you on the sand, though further from the old town. The atlas does not yet carry hotel pins for Valencia, so book on the usual sites and read recent reviews for the property.

## The old town, the markets, and the Turia

The **old town** is the heart of Valencia, a walkable quarter of squares and lanes around **Valencia Cathedral**, which claims to hold the Holy Grail and is topped by the climbable Miguelete bell tower. Nearby, the **Lonja de la Seda**, the Gothic silk exchange, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Two market halls anchor the city's food life. The [Mercat Central](/pins/mercat-central-de-valencia) is one of the largest covered markets in Europe, a Modernista hall packed with produce, meat, and seafood stalls. The [Mercat de Colón](/pins/mercat-de-colon), a beautifully restored Modernista market building, now runs as a stylish food-and-cafe hall.

The **Turia Gardens** are Valencia's defining feature: after a catastrophic 1957 flood, the river was diverted and its old bed turned into a nine-kilometer park that curves through the city. Walking or cycling it links the old town to the **City of Arts and Sciences**, the white futuristic complex of museum, aquarium, and opera house designed largely by Santiago Calatrava at the park's seaward end.

## Where to eat

Valencia eats on rice above all. Paella is a Valencian dish, traditionally made with rabbit, chicken, and beans and eaten at lunch, and the city takes it seriously. For the real thing, look to restaurants that specialize in rice, or head to the Albufera lagoon where the rice is grown. Beyond rice, the markets and the Ruzafa scene cover the rest.
