Verona is a Roman arena, a summer opera season, and the Romeo and Juliet myth, packed into a UNESCO old town on a bend of the Adige. A day or two covers it, slotted neatly between Venice and Milan. Here's how I'd spend it.

## How long do I need?

One day covers the core: the Roman Arena, the old town squares, and Juliet's House. A second day adds Castelvecchio, the churches, and a slower pace, or a half-day out to Lake Garda.

Verona sits on the main rail line between Venice and Milan, so it works well as a one-night stop on a wider northern Italy trip rather than a long stay.

## How do I get to Verona?

Verona is on Italy's main northern rail line. Fast trains reach it from **Venice** and **Milan** in a little over an hour, and from Florence and Rome in two to three. **Verona Porta Nuova** station is a short bus ride or a 20-minute walk from the old town.

Verona has its own small airport, and the airports of Venice, Milan, and Bergamo are all within reach. Once in the old town, everything is walkable.

## Where should I stay?

Stay in the **old town**, the historic center inside the river bend. From there the Arena, the main squares, and the restaurants are all on foot, and the station is a short bus ride or walk away.

## The Arena and the old town

The **Verona Arena** is the centerpiece, a first-century Roman amphitheatre standing in the middle of the city, one of the best-preserved anywhere. It is open by day as a monument, and in summer it becomes the stage for a major open-air opera festival, so book well ahead if seeing a performance there is the plan.

The old town spreads out from it. **Piazza delle Erbe**, the long market square on the site of the Roman forum, is the heart of the center, and the **Piazza dei Signori** beside it is the more formal civic square. **Juliet's House**, the Casa di Giulietta, draws the crowds to its 20th-century balcony, a piece of romantic tourism attached to a fictional character. At the river's edge, **Castelvecchio** is the red-brick medieval castle with its fortified bridge, now an art museum, and **Ponte Pietra** is the Roman stone bridge upstream.

## Where to eat

Verona eats on Veneto cooking: risotto, the bitter-green and meat dishes of the region, polenta, and the local Valpolicella and Amarone wines.
