[Venlo](/cities/venlo) is a town of about 100,000 people in the southeastern Dutch province of Limburg, set on the River Maas and directly against the German border. It is a compact, easygoing town with a Renaissance town hall, a walkable old center, and a good local food-and-cafe scene. Half a day to a day covers it. This guide covers getting in, where to stay, what to see, and where to eat.

## How long do I need?

Half a day to a day covers Venlo. The old center is compact, and the town hall, the historic streets, and the market square can be seen in a few hours, with a relaxed lunch stretching it to a full day.

Venlo works best as a stop rather than a base, slotted into a wider trip through Limburg, or as a cross-border day out from the German side, where it has long been a familiar shopping town.

## How do I get to Venlo?

Venlo sits on the rail line between the Dutch Randstad and Germany. Direct trains connect it with **Eindhoven** and the western Netherlands, and with **Düsseldorf** and the German network across the border. Venlo station is a short walk from the old center.

The nearest airports are **Eindhoven** and **Düsseldorf**. Once in town, the center is small and walkable.

## Where should I stay?

For most visitors Venlo is a day stop and does not need an overnight. If you do stay, the **old center** around the market square is the natural base, walkable to everything and to the station.

## What to see in Venlo

The landmark is the **town hall**, the Stadhuis, a Renaissance building of the late 16th century standing on the market square, the finest single sight in town. Around it, the **old center** is a walkable grid of historic streets, shops, and cafes, partly rebuilt after heavy damage in the Second World War.

**St. Martin's Church** is the tall Gothic church anchoring the skyline. The **River Maas** runs along the edge of the center, with a riverside walk. Venlo is not a town of major museums, and the pleasure of it is a slow wander and a long lunch.

## Where to eat

Venlo eats on Limburg cooking, a softer, more southern Dutch style with its own pastries and a strong cafe tradition, plus the easy cross-border German influence.
