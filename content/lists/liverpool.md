[Liverpool](/cities/liverpool) has more going for it than its tourist reputation lets on, a compact, walkable port city with one of Britain's best waterfronts and a real food-and-bar scene under the Beatles industry. Two days, three if you want a football match. Here's how I'd do it.

## How many days do I need?

Two days is the shape of a first visit. The first day goes to the waterfront and the dock museums, the second to the cathedrals, a Beatles afternoon, and the eating-and-drinking streets. A third day buys a match at Anfield, the deeper Beatles tour out to the suburban homes, or simply a slower pace.

The center is small and flat, and almost everything in this guide is within a twenty-minute walk. Liverpool also pairs well with Manchester, about an hour east by train, if you want a wider northwest England trip.

## How do I get to Liverpool?

**Avanti West Coast trains** run from London Euston to **Liverpool Lime Street** in about 2 hours 15 minutes, direct and roughly hourly. Advance fares booked ahead are far cheaper than walk-up. Lime Street station sits in the city center, a short walk from everything here.

**Liverpool John Lennon Airport (LPL)** is about 12 km south of the center and handles mostly European and domestic flights. Bus 86A and the 500 airport service run into the city. For long-haul, **Manchester Airport** has the wider network and is about an hour away by direct train.

Inside Liverpool you do not need transport. The whole guide is walkable, and the Merseyrail trains cover the few things that are not.

## Where should I stay?

Stay in the city center. From a central base the waterfront, the cathedrals, the Beatles sites, and the food streets are all on foot.

The area around **Liverpool ONE** and the commercial district is the convenient, central choice, walkable to the docks and the shopping. The **Ropewalks**, the grid of old warehouse streets around Bold Street, puts you in the middle of the bars and restaurants and is the lively, later base. The **waterfront** near the Albert Dock is the scenic option, quieter at night. ## What is on the waterfront?

The Mersey waterfront is the set-piece of the city and the right place to start.

The **Royal Albert Dock** is the restored complex of red-brick dock warehouses, now home to a run of museums, bars, and restaurants. The **Merseyside Maritime Museum** and the **International Slavery Museum** sit here and are free, and the **Tate Liverpool** brings the modern-art collection north, though it is in a phased refurbishment, so check before you plan around it.

The **Pier Head**, just north, is where the city faces the river. The **Three Graces**, the trio of grand Edwardian buildings, stand over the water: the **Royal Liver Building** with its two clock towers and the mythical Liver Birds on top, the **Cunard Building**, and the **Port of Liverpool Building**. The waterfront held UNESCO World Heritage status from 2004 until 2021, when it was delisted over new dockland development.

For the view from above, [Radio City Tower](/pins/radio-city-tower-recently-viewed), the city's 1960s observation tower at St John's Beacon, gives a 360-degree look over Liverpool and the river.

## Are the Beatles sites worth it?

That depends on how much you care, and Liverpool lets you pick the level.

The free, city-center version takes about an hour: **Mathew Street**, the lane that holds the rebuilt **Cavern Club** where the band played early shows, plus the statues and plaques around it. The paid, self-paced version is the **Beatles Story**, the museum at the Albert Dock.

The deeper version is the suburbs. **Penny Lane** and **Strawberry Field** are out in south Liverpool, and the **National Trust** runs guided tours of the childhood homes of John Lennon and Paul McCartney, which sell out and need booking ahead. A half-day Beatles tour by minibus is the usual way to string the suburban sites together.

## The two cathedrals

Liverpool has two cathedrals, both 20th-century, both at opposite ends of the same street, and the pair is best seen together.

**Liverpool Cathedral** is the Anglican one, designed by Giles Gilbert Scott, the largest cathedral in Britain and one of the largest in the world. It is a vast sandstone Gothic-revival building, and the tower visit gives another high view over the city.

The **Metropolitan Cathedral of Christ the King** is the Roman Catholic one, a circular 1960s modernist building topped by a lantern tower, known locally as "Paddy's Wigwam." The interior, lit through colored glass, is the opposite experience to the Anglican cathedral. The road between them is called Hope Street, which the city enjoys pointing out.

## Where should I eat and drink?

Liverpool's food is better than visitors expect, and most of it clusters in two areas. **Bold Street** is the independent restaurant strip, a long run of small kitchens covering most cuisines. The **Baltic Triangle**, the former industrial quarter south of the center, is the bars-and-street-food district, busiest in the evening.
