Palma is the capital of Mallorca, and most visitors treat it as nothing but the airport for the island's beaches. That skips a real Spanish city: a giant seafront cathedral, a walkable old town, a strong restaurant scene. Two or three days for Palma itself. Here's how I'd spend them.

## How many days do I need?

Two days covers the city itself: the cathedral, the old town, Bellver Castle, and the seafront, with time to eat properly. If you are using Palma as a base for the rest of Mallorca, add days for the mountains and the beaches.

The point worth making is that Palma is a destination, not just a transfer point. Treated as a two or three-day city break, it holds its own against better-known Spanish cities.

## How do I get in from the airport?

**Palma de Mallorca Airport (PMI)** sits about 8 km east of the city. The **A1 airport bus** runs directly into the center and along the seafront, frequently and cheaply, and is the easy way in. A taxi takes around 20 minutes.

The airport is one of the busiest in Spain through the summer, so allow extra time on the way out. Inside Palma the center is walkable, and buses cover the rest.

## Where should I stay?

Stay in the **old town**, the dense quarter of lanes behind the cathedral. It is the most walkable base, full of restaurants and small squares, and central to everything in this guide.

The **seafront and marina** area is the alternative, with sea views and easy airport-bus access. The atlas does not yet carry hotel pins for Palma, so book on the usual sites and read recent reviews for the property.

## What to see in Palma

**Palma Cathedral**, known as **La Seu**, is the landmark, a vast Gothic cathedral rising straight off the seafront, with one of the largest rose windows in the world and an interior reworked in part by Antoni Gaudí. It is the first thing you see arriving by sea or air, and the right place to start.

Beside it stands the **Royal Palace of La Almudaina**, the old Moorish-built fortress-palace still used for state occasions. The **old town** behind them is a walkable maze of patio houses, courtyards, and small squares, with the leafy **Passeig del Born** as its main avenue and the small domed **Arab Baths** a surviving trace of the Moorish city. Above the city, the round **Bellver Castle** is an unusual circular Gothic fortress with a wide view over the bay.

## Beyond the city

Palma works as a base for the rest of Mallorca. The **Serra de Tramuntana**, the UNESCO-listed mountain range along the northwest coast, holds the stone village of **Valldemossa** and the harbor town of **Port de Sóller**. The vintage wooden **train to Sóller** is a scenic ride through the mountains and a popular half-day. The island's beaches and coves spread around the rest of the coast, most within an hour's drive.

## Where to eat

Palma eats on Mallorcan cooking: the spiral *ensaïmada* pastry, *pa amb oli* bread with oil and toppings, island seafood and rice dishes, and a growing modern restaurant scene in the old town.
