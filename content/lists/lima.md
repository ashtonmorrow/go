[Lima](/cities/lima) gets a single night from most travelers, a layover on the way to Cusco, and that sells it badly short. It is one of the best eating cities in the world, strung along desert cliffs over the Pacific. Give it two or three days. Here's how I'd spend them.

## How many days do I need?

Two or three days is the right amount of Lima. That covers the colonial historic center, the clifftop neighborhoods, a museum or two, and a real run at the food.

Lima is often treated as a layover on the way to the Inca sights, and a single night does not do it justice. Built in as a couple of unhurried days, it stands on its own, mostly on the strength of the eating.

## How do I get in from the airport?

**Jorge Chávez International Airport (LIM)** sits in Callao, northwest of the city, and opened a large new terminal in 2025. Depending on traffic it is 30 to 60 minutes to Miraflores, where most visitors stay.

Use an **official airport taxi**, booked at the desk inside the terminal, or an **Uber**, rather than accepting a ride from a tout in the arrivals hall. Agree the route and the rough fare before you set off.

## Where should I stay?

For a first visit, the choice is between three central districts, and Miraflores is the default.

| Area | Why pick it | Trade-off |
|---|---|---|
| **Miraflores** | Safe, walkable, on the Pacific cliffs, close to parks, markets, and the coast path | The most touristed district, and priced accordingly |
| **Barranco** | The smaller bohemian neighborhood, arty, full of bars and restaurants | A little further from the center, quieter by day |
| **San Isidro** | Upscale and calm, the financial district, leafy and residential | Less street life, more of a business base |

The colonial historic center is better visited than slept in. The atlas does not yet carry hotel pins for Lima, so book on the usual sites and read recent reviews for the property.

## The historic center and the neighborhoods

The **historic center**, the old colonial core, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and a half-day most Miraflores-based visitors skip. The **Plaza Mayor** is the heart of it, fronted by Lima Cathedral and the Government Palace, and the nearby **San Francisco Monastery** is known for its bone-lined catacombs. [Plaza San Martín](/pins/plaza-san-martin), a grand early-20th-century square, anchors the other end of the center.

**Miraflores** is the clifftop modern district, built around Parque Kennedy and the **Malecón**, the long landscaped path along the cliff edge above the Pacific. [Larcomar](/pins/larcomar) is the shopping and dining complex built into the cliff face there, with the ocean below. **Barranco**, just south, is the small bohemian quarter, with the Bridge of Sighs and a strong bar and gallery scene.

## The museums and the markets

Two museums are worth the trip. The [Larco Museum](/pins/larco-museum), set in a white colonial mansion, holds one of the best pre-Columbian collections in Peru, including a famous gallery of erotic ceramics. The [Huaca Pucllana Site Museum](/pins/huaca-pucllana-site-museum) is built around an adobe pyramid that rises in the middle of residential Miraflores, a pre-Inca ceremonial site you can tour and dine beside.

For shopping, three craft markets cluster in Miraflores: the [Inka Market](/pins/inka-market), [Mercado 28](/pins/mercado-28-miraflores), and the [Miraflores Indian Market](/pins/miraflores-indian-market), all good for alpaca textiles, ceramics, and souvenirs, with prices that move if you ask.

## Where should I eat?

This is the reason to give Lima time. Peruvian food runs on **ceviche**, the cured raw fish that is close to a national dish, **Nikkei** cooking from the Japanese-Peruvian community, **Chifa**, the Chinese-Peruvian style, and a fine-dining scene that puts Lima restaurants near the top of the world rankings year after year. The pisco sour is the drink.

Around it, Lima's everyday eating, the ceviche counters and the *menú* lunches, is where the city is just as good for far less money.
