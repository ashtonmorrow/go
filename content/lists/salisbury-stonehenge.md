Stonehenge is the Neolithic stone circle on Salisbury Plain in Wiltshire, southwest England, and the most visited prehistoric site in the country. [Salisbury](/cities/salisbury) is the cathedral city about nine miles south, and it is the practical base for the visit: it has the train station, the food, and a cathedral worth a half-day on its own. Most trips pair the two. This guide covers how to get to the stones, the paid English Heritage visit, the free walking approach Mike has used, and what to do in Salisbury itself.

## How do I get to Stonehenge?

There is no train to the stones. You get to Salisbury by train, then cover the last leg by bus or car.

**The train to Salisbury** runs frequently. From London Waterloo it takes about 90 minutes on South Western Railway. From Bath Spa it is about an hour on Great Western Railway, sometimes direct and sometimes with a change at Westbury. Bath is a good base for this trip, and that connection is one of the better reasons to stay in Bath overnight rather than day-tripping it from London.

**The Stonehenge Tour bus** is the hop-on-hop-off service run by Salisbury Reds. It leaves from outside Salisbury station, runs every 30 minutes in summer and less often in winter, and takes about 30 minutes to the stones. It also stops at Old Sarum, the Iron Age hill fort, on the way. The standard ticket bundles the bus fare with English Heritage admission to Stonehenge, which is the simplest way to handle the whole visit on one purchase.

**By car**, Stonehenge is just off the A303, about 35 minutes from Salisbury. English Heritage runs the visitor center with its own car park. Book a timed entry slot in advance in the high season, because [Stonehenge](/pins/stonehenge-avebury-and-associated-sites) is part of a UNESCO World Heritage site and entry is capped through the day.

## Can you see Stonehenge for free?

Yes, if you have a car. A public byway crosses Salisbury Plain near the stones and gives a free view from outside the English Heritage paid perimeter, on the Larkhill side.

The way Mike has done it: drive to [The Packhorse](/pins/the-packhorse-larkhill), the pub near the Larkhill military garrison, park around there, have a pint, then walk over the plain to the stones. It is roughly a half-hour walk on the byway. You cannot walk the full circle around the stones from this side, because the inner perimeter is fenced, but the view from the byway is real and the whole thing costs nothing beyond the pint.

This is worth knowing if you are skeptical of the paid visitor-center experience, or have already done it once and want a different angle. It needs a car. There is no useful bus to the Larkhill side.

## Salisbury Cathedral and the Magna Carta

[Salisbury Cathedral](/pins/salisbury-cathedral) has the tallest church spire in Britain, about 123 meters, and it is the reason to give the city a half-day rather than treating it as a bus interchange. The cathedral was built in the 13th century in a single architectural style, which is rare, and the close around it is one of the largest in England.

The [Magna Carta Chapter House](/pins/magna-carta-chapter-house) holds one of only four surviving 1215 originals of the Magna Carta, displayed inside the cathedral's 13th-century Chapter House. Admission to the cathedral covers it. This is the best-preserved of the four copies and the practical reason to go inside rather than just photographing the spire.

## Where to eat in Salisbury

Salisbury eats far better than anything around the stones, so plan the meal in town and treat the site as a side trip. The city center is compact and most of these are a short walk apart.

[The Haunch of Venison](/pins/the-haunch-of-venison) is the 14th-century pub near the market square, low-beamed and centuries old, good for a drink and a traditional plate. [The Pheasant Inn](/pins/the-pheasant-inn) is the other pub option. For sit-down dinners, [Côte](/pins/cote-salisbury) is the reliable French bistro chain, [Byron](/pins/byron-salisbury) does burgers, and [wagamama](/pins/wagamama-salisbury) covers the fast Asian-bowl option. [Baroushka](/pins/baroushka) and [Chapter 69](/pins/chapter-69) round out the dinner list, and [Culture Coffee](/pins/culture-coffee) is the daytime coffee stop.

## Parks and a riverside walk

Salisbury sits where five rivers meet, and the water meadows southwest of the cathedral give the classic view of the spire across open grass, the one John Constable painted. [Harnham Gate](/pins/harnham-gate) is the old gate into the cathedral close on the Harnham side, the start of the meadow walk. [Churchill Gardens](/pins/churchill-gardens) is the riverside park near the center, an easy green stop between the station and the cathedral.
